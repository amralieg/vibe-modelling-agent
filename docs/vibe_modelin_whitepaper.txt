VIBE DATA MODELING: A WHITE PAPER

NOTE: All examples in this paper are illustrative and industry-neutral. When
applying these concepts, always adapt terminology, domain names, product names,
and entity names to the specific industry and business being modeled.

Data modeling is about organizing data. That is all it has ever been about.

Whether the result is called an industry data model, a business data model, a canonical model, or an enterprise schema, the underlying work is the same: decide what concepts the business cares about, decide how those concepts relate, and decide where each piece of data lives. Think of it like an image. A photograph of a tree looks the same whether it is stored as a JPEG, a PNG, or a TIFF. The format is the container; the tree is the content. In data modeling, the methodology is the format. The content -- the actual business, its processes, its relationships -- is what matters.

Vibe data modeling is a faster, more natural way to build that content.

It does not produce an industry data model -- a generic template that approximates a sector. It produces a business data model: a structure that is specific, authentic, and representative of how one particular organization actually works. It is built through natural language, refined through iteration, and governed by one hundred and sixty-two enforceable rules that ensure consistency, accuracy, and production readiness.

The process has two distinct steps.

Step one is the base model -- version zero, the seed. The modeler describes their business, and the system generates a complete data model by following a single decomposition path: Organization to Divisions to Domains to Products to Attributes. The organization is segmented into three divisions. Each division is broken into domains. Each domain is filled with products (tables). Each product is filled with attributes (columns). Then everything is linked together -- within domains, across domains -- and validated against every rule in the catalog. The output is the seed model: a fully linked, fully tagged, physically deployed starting point. It is not perfect -- no first draft ever is -- but it is comprehensive, consistent, and honest about where it falls short.

Step two is vibe modeling -- the iteration. The modeler looks at the seed and describes, in natural language, what they want to change. "Add a loyalty domain." "Rename mobile_number to phone_number everywhere." "The billing domain needs a collections workflow." The system reads the modeler's intent, classifies the type of change, executes with precision, and validates with the same rigor as the original build. Each vibe creates a new version -- v1, v2, v3 -- each one closer to the ideal. No version is overwritten. The model evolves iteratively toward the true representation of the business -- not in months, but in minutes.

This paper walks through both steps. Each builds on the one before.



================================================================================

STEP 1 -- THE BASE MODEL

================================================================================


The base model is version zero -- the seed. It is the initial information gathering and generation pass that produces the first complete representation of the business. Everything that follows in step two will iterate on this seed, refining it version by version. But the seed itself must be solid.

The generation follows one decomposition path, and it never deviates:

    Organization → Divisions → Domains → Products → Attributes

The modeler describes the organization. The system segments it into three divisions. It breaks each division into domains. It fills each domain with products (tables). It fills each product with attributes (columns). Then it links everything -- within domains, across domains -- validates the result, deploys it, and assesses where it falls short. The output is version zero: a complete, self-aware starting point that knows exactly what the next iteration should fix.



1.1 -- KNOWING THE BUSINESS BEFORE MODELING IT


Every data model begins with a question: what does this business do?

The answer seems obvious to the people who work there. But obvious to a human is not obvious to a system. The system needs structure: not just the company name and a tagline, but a layered portrait that captures the business at multiple levels of detail.

The modeler provides this portrait through a business context file. It contains three things. The first is business information -- the company's name, a rich description of what it does, the industry it operates in, the core business processes it runs every day, the organizational divisions it uses, the data domains it already knows about, the jargon its people speak, the operational systems that currently hold its data, and the governing body that regulates its industry.

For a retail company, the business processes might be "merchandise planning, inventory replenishment, order fulfillment, customer loyalty management, and supply chain optimization." The jargon might include SKU, POS, GMROI, shrinkage. The governing body might be PCI DSS for payment security and local consumer protection authorities. For a healthcare organization, the processes would be "patient admissions, clinical documentation, claims processing, pharmacy dispensing, and quality reporting." The jargon would be ICD, CPT, DRG, HIPAA. The point is: every industry has its own language, and the system must speak it.

The second element is model conventions -- the formatting rules that govern every name, type, and tag in the model: how dates are formatted, what type primary keys use, how booleans are represented, what classification levels apply to sensitive data, and any naming prefixes or suffixes the organization requires.

The third is vibe modeling instructions -- a free-text field where, in step two, the modeler will describe changes in natural language. For a new base model, this field is empty.

Once the modeler provides this context, the system does something a traditional tool cannot: it enriches the input. Acting as a seasoned business analyst with deep industry knowledge, the system expands every dimension. If the modeler wrote "retail" for industry alignment, the enrichment expands that into a precise characterization: omni-channel retailer, specialty retailer, or e-commerce-first brand. If the modeler listed three business processes, the enrichment expands them into specific, ordered workflows: "billing" becomes "order capture, payment processing, invoice generation, refund handling, and revenue recognition." Each expanded process implies specific data entities that the model will need.

This enrichment is not decoration. It is the foundation that every subsequent step will consult. A model that does not know what a SKU is will generate useless column names in retail. A model that cannot distinguish an ICD code from a CPT code will fail in healthcare. The enriched context ensures the model speaks the business's language from its very first table.

Before moving on, one thing must be clear: nothing that follows is random. Every step in the model's lifecycle is governed by a catalog of one hundred and sixty-two documented rules, organized into twelve groups covering naming conventions, semantic deduplication, foreign keys, primary keys, normalization, domain structure, data types, tags, graph topology, quality, product design, and semantic distinction. Each rule has an ID, a rationale, and an example. They are enforceable constraints, not suggestions. The modeler provides the soul; the rules provide the skeleton. The complete catalog appears in the Appendix, and rules are referenced by ID throughout this paper.

And every step -- from this enrichment to the last foreign key constraint -- follows the same disciplined cycle: generate, validate against the rules, and if validation fails, feed the errors back and retry. This four-beat rhythm is the heartbeat of quality. Nothing moves forward until it passes scrutiny.



1.2 -- SEGMENTING THE ORGANIZATION INTO THREE DIVISIONS


Before a single domain is named, the system establishes the organizational scaffolding. Every business, regardless of industry, operates in three concentric rings of activity. Understanding these rings is the first architectural decision.

The innermost ring is Operations -- what the business physically does to function. In retail: warehouse management, inventory control, order fulfillment, supply chain logistics. In healthcare: patient admissions, clinical procedures, pharmacy dispensing. In manufacturing: production scheduling, quality control, equipment maintenance. The test question is direct: can the business operate for one day without this division's data? If the answer is no, it belongs in Operations.

The second ring is Business -- who the business serves and how it earns revenue. Customer management, billing, product catalogs, sales and contracts. The test: does this area directly generate revenue or directly serve customers? If yes, it belongs in Business.

The outermost ring is Corporate -- the supporting scaffolding. Marketing campaigns, regulatory compliance, human resources, corporate finance. These enable the business but do not directly generate revenue. The test: could the business survive without this division's data for a week? If yes, it is probably Corporate.

This three-ring structure is not a suggestion. It is a constraint (Rule G06-R001) that ensures the model is built from the inside out -- operations first, because they are the foundation; business second, because revenue depends on operations; corporate last, because it supports both. Every domain that will be created in the next step must be classified into exactly one of these three divisions. This prevents the common failure mode of building a model that is heavy on HR, finance, and legal domains while starving the operational core that actually runs the business.



1.3 -- SEGMENTING DIVISIONS INTO DOMAINS


The three divisions are now established -- Operations, Business, Corporate. But a division is too broad to model directly. No one builds a database called "Operations." The next step is to break each division into domains -- the actual modeling units that will hold tables, columns, and relationships.

A domain is not a database. It is not a department. It is a bounded context -- a coherent region within a division that owns a distinct set of concepts and speaks its own dialect. Think of each division as a continent, and domains as the countries within it. Operations might contain "production," "logistics," and "maintenance." Business might contain "customer," "billing," and "product." Corporate might contain "finance," "compliance," and "workforce." Each domain belongs to exactly one division, and each inherits that division's character: Operations domains deal with what the business does, Business domains deal with who it serves, Corporate domains deal with how it runs.


What is a domain?

A domain answers the question: "Within this division, what specific area of the business does this data belong to?" In a retailer's Operations division, "inventory" is a domain -- it owns the concepts of stock levels, warehousing, and replenishment. In the same company's Business division, "billing" is a domain -- it owns the concepts of invoices, charges, and payments. Each domain owns a set of concepts that other domains may reference but do not own. The customer domain owns who the customer is. The billing domain owns what the customer owes. The operations domain owns what infrastructure or processes exist. The domain name should always use terminology natural to the specific industry being modeled.


The Org Chart Test.

Every domain must pass a simple reality check (Rule G06-R016): would a real department or team with this name exist in the organization? "Finance" passes -- there is a finance department. "Customer" passes. "Inventory" passes for a retailer. But "utilities" fails -- no one works in the "Utilities department." "Processing" fails -- it is too vague to be meaningful. If you cannot imagine a recruiter posting jobs in that department, it is not a real domain.

Generic catch-all names are forbidden (Rule G06-R014): "services," "platform," "shared," "common," "core," "misc," and "admin" are not domains. They are buckets where unclear thinking goes to hide. And analytics-oriented names are equally forbidden (Rule G06-R015): no "analytics," "reporting," "intelligence," "metrics," "kpi," or "datawarehouse" domain. The base model captures what happened -- raw operational and transactional facts at the Silver layer of the medallion architecture. Derived analytics belong in the Gold layer above.


Boundaries and rules.

Because domains are children of divisions, the generation order follows the division hierarchy. Domain selection uses an interleaving algorithm (Rule G06-R005): one from Operations, then one from Business, alternating to keep the model balanced. Only after both divisions have at least two domains does the model consider any Corporate domain (Rule G06-R004). Corporate domains can never exceed twenty percent of the total (Rule G06-R002). This ensures the model is weighted toward the operational and revenue-generating core, not the back-office.

Domain names must be exactly one word, lowercase, singular, and no longer than twenty characters (Rules G01-R002 and G01-R003). "customer" is valid. "customer_service" is not -- two words. "customers" is not -- plural.

Each domain receives a clear description of at least twenty characters explaining its business purpose. No two domains may have descriptions with more than seventy percent word overlap -- because if two descriptions sound the same, the domains probably are the same and should be merged.

The system does not rely on a single judgment. It solicits four independent domain proposals generated with different reasoning styles, then a fifth, senior judge reviews all four side by side. The judge looks for consensus, unique insights, and redundancy. If one proposal calls it "customer" and another calls it "client," the judge recognizes these as the same concept and picks one canonical name, enforcing the Single Source of Truth principle (Rule G06-R006): each business concept can be owned by exactly one domain.

The result is a set of domains, each classified into one of the three divisions. The continents are divided into countries. The borders are set.



1.4 -- FILLING DOMAINS WITH TABLES


With domains established, the model enters its most prolific phase: filling each domain with data products -- the tables that will hold the business's data.

All domains are populated simultaneously. For each domain, the model generates tables that naturally belong there. In "customer": profile, account, address, contact, preference, segment. In "billing": invoice, payment, charge, credit_note, dispute. In "operations": equipment, work_order, schedule, inspection, facility.


Business first.

Every table must represent a genuine business concept that a domain expert would recognize. Technical artifacts like "etl_job_log" or "batch_control" are plumbing -- they belong in operational schemas, not the business model (Rule G12-R001). Analytics tables like "churn_prediction" or "revenue_analysis" are derived insights that belong in the Gold layer (Rule G12-R002). Speculative content like "blockchain_hash" or "quantum_encryption_key" represents what the business might do someday, not what it does today (Rule G12-R004). All three are turned away.


Single Source of Truth.

After all domains have their tables, the model runs global semantic deduplication. It scans every table across every domain, looking for duplicates -- tables that represent the same business concept under different names. "invoice" and "bill" are the same thing. "customer" and "client" are the same thing. The SSOT principle (Rule G02-R001) demands that each concept has exactly one authoritative home.

But deduplication is conservative. Not everything that looks similar is the same. Different lifecycle stages are not duplicates (Rule G02-R006): a "quote" and an "order" represent different stages. Different granularity levels are not duplicates (Rule G02-R007): individual usage events and monthly summaries serve different purposes. Parent and child entities are not duplicates (Rule G02-R008): "order" groups items, "order_item" details them. When genuine duplicates are found, merging is preferred over deletion (Rule G02-R009), and a discriminator column preserves context.


Semantic first.

Tables are classified by nature (Rule G07-R008). Master data (customer, product) describes what entities are -- authoritative records that change slowly. Reference data (country, currency) provides static lookups. Transactional data (order, payment) captures what happened. Association data (enrollment) represents many-to-many relationships. Each table is also classified as "core" or "helper" (Rule G12-R007). Core tables are essential, directly queried by stakeholders. Each domain should have one to three core tables maximum (Rule G12-R009) -- because if everything is core, nothing is.

Table naming is strict: one to three words, lowercase, under thirty characters (Rule G01-R004). Names never repeat the domain name as a prefix (Rule G01-R005) -- in "customer," the table is "account," not "customer_account," because the full path "customer.account" already provides context.

Each table receives a primary key following a sacred convention (Rules G04-R001, G01-R007): the table name followed by "_id." The "customer" table gets "customer_id." When you see "customer_id" in any table, in any domain, you immediately know it references the customer table. The model is self-documenting by design.



1.5 -- FILLING TABLES WITH ATTRIBUTES


Every table now has a name, a classification, and a primary key. But a table without columns is just a label. This step gives each table its substance.

Attribute generation runs for every table in parallel. The model knows what each table type demands (Rule G12-R018). A person entity without name and contact fields is a ghost. An account without type, status, and key dates is an empty shell. A transaction without a date or an amount is a record of nothing.


No trivial attributes.

Every column must earn its place (Rule G12-R017). The litmus test: "What specific business question does this column answer?" If the column "order_date" answers "When was this order placed?" it stays. If "notes_field_3" answers nothing specific, it goes. No filler. No padding. No speculative columns.

Pre-calculated metrics like "average_monthly_spend" or "churn_score" are refused (Rule G12-R003). These are derived insights. The base model stores raw ingredients -- individual charges, individual transactions -- and lets the analytics layer compute the totals and scores.


No duplicates.

Column names never repeat the table name as a prefix, except for the primary key (Rule G01-R006). In "account," the column is "balance," not "account_balance." Column names prioritize clarity over brevity (Rule G01-R013): always "mean_time_between_failures," never "mtbf." Industry jargon earns a place when universally recognized (Rule G01-R015) -- "sku" in retail, "iban" in banking, "icd_code" in healthcare -- but lazy abbreviations like "cust_nm" for "customer_name" never do.

Data types are inferred from naming patterns (Rule G07-R011): "_id" columns get integers, "_date" gets DATE, "_at" gets TIMESTAMP, monetary suffixes get DECIMAL. No complex types like ARRAY or MAP are allowed (Rule G07-R002) -- nested data is modeled as separate, normalized tables.

Within each table, columns follow a strict seating arrangement (Rule G12-R021): primary key first, then foreign keys, then business attributes, then housekeeping columns, and finally history-tracking columns. This ordering makes every table scannable and predictable.



1.6 -- IN-DOMAIN LINKING


Tables exist. Columns exist. But without relationships, they are a collection of isolated spreadsheets. The web of foreign key relationships is what transforms tables into a model.

In-domain linking is where that web begins, and it starts inside each domain because the relationships here are the most natural and the most obvious. Tables that belong to the same business area are almost certainly connected.

Consider the "customer" domain with tables for profile, account, address, preference, and segment. An account belongs to a profile. An address belongs to a profile. A preference belongs to a profile. These are not arbitrary connections -- they represent the real-world truth that a customer's account, address, and preferences all belong to that customer.

Why in-domain first? Because in the real organization, the people who work in billing mostly work with other billing tables. The people who manage the customer domain mostly join customer tables to other customer tables. In-domain relationships are the high-frequency joins -- the ones that analysts run hundreds of times a day. Getting them right is the foundation that cross-domain linking will build on.

The rules governing foreign keys serve one purpose: preventing relationships that would cause problems in production. Every foreign key must point to the actual primary key of its target (Rule G03-R001), data types must match (Rule G03-R004), and foreign keys always follow the child-to-parent direction (Rule G09-R005): "order.customer_id" is correct; "customer.latest_order_id" is wrong. Bidirectional keys are forbidden (Rule G03-R003) because they create cycles. Self-references are blocked unless hierarchical (Rule G03-R002): "category.parent_category_id" is legitimate; a random self-reference is not.

When a foreign key is established, normalization follows automatically (Rule G03-R010). If "order" gains "customer_id," then redundant columns like "customer_name" and "customer_email" are removed -- because those values are now accessible through the join. The order is always: add the key first, remove the redundancy second.

Before linking even begins, the model runs a normalization integrity check on every table in parallel. It finds orphaned foreign key columns -- columns ending in "_id" with no formal link declared -- and either connects them to their obvious targets or flags them for resolution. It also finds denormalization violations: columns that copy data from another table, like "customer_name" appearing on "invoice" when "customer_id" already exists (Rule G05-R001). Redundant columns are removed; the join path replaces them.

After the domain-level linking pass, a batch semantic resolution step sweeps the entire model, collecting every remaining unlinked "_id" column and resolving it in one comprehensive pass.



1.7 -- CROSS-DOMAIN LINKING AND PAIRWISE COMPARISON


If in-domain linking connects the rooms of a house, cross-domain linking connects the houses into a city. This is where the model becomes a true enterprise data mesh.

The organization is one body. The billing department serves the customer department's customers. The operations department's infrastructure supports the product department's offerings. The support department resolves issues that originate in the operations domain. These connections must exist in the data model, or cross-domain analytics -- the entire reason an enterprise model exists -- becomes impossible.

The system examines every unique pair of domains. For a model with seven domains, that is twenty-one pairs. For twenty-one domains, that is two hundred and ten pairs. Each pair is examined in parallel: how does billing connect to customer? How does support connect to operations? How does logistics connect to procurement?

No domain may be an island (Rule G09-R004). Every domain must connect to at least one other through foreign key relationships. An isolated domain is a data silo that cannot participate in cross-domain analytics. An analyst asking "which products generate the most billing disputes?" needs to traverse from product through billing to get the answer. If those domains are not connected, the question is unanswerable.

Before creating any cross-domain link, the model performs an identity check on each domain pair. Are these two domains actually the same domain accidentally split? If they share five or more products with the same names and their functional overlap exceeds seventy percent, they are flagged as identical and recommended for merger.

Cross-domain many-to-many relationships are treated with extreme conservatism (Rule G12-R013). The model expects at most zero to one per model. The tests are stringent: is the relationship genuinely bidirectional? Does the business track at least two attributes about the relationship itself? Does the business have a name for this relationship? "Customers subscribe to products with terms, dates, and tiers" is a genuine many-to-many. "An order belongs to one customer" is a simple foreign key.

Throughout this phase, the model enforces its most important structural law (Rule G09-R001): the data model must form a Directed Acyclic Graph. Follow foreign key relationships from any table, and you must never arrive back at the starting table. Cycles make it impossible to determine insertion order, break referential integrity, and create infinite loops. When cycles are detected, the model breaks the weakest link: convenience references like "latest_order_id" or "primary_invoice_id" are always broken first (Rule G09-R007); core structural relationships like "order_item.order_id" are never broken (Rule G09-R008).



1.8 -- QUALITY CHECKS, DEPLOYMENT, AND SELF-ASSESSMENT


Everything built so far -- domains, tables, columns, relationships -- has been constructed in stages, each following its own rules. But has the whole held together? A city built neighborhood by neighborhood can still have mismatched roads, dead ends, and buildings that violate the zoning code. This step holds the entire model up to the light.

The quality assurance suite is comprehensive. It begins at the edges: empty domains are removed (Rule G11-R023), small tables are evaluated for merging (Rule G12-R026), and every table is verified to have a primary key (Rule G04-R001).

Then it turns inward to the structural backbone. The model identifies its core products -- the tables fundamental to the business -- and marks them as protected: they cannot be merged, removed, or relocated. A fresh semantic deduplication scan runs at the global level, because linking and normalization may have revealed new overlaps. Global product name conflicts across domains are investigated: "customer.device" (a phone) and "iot.device" (a sensor) share a name but represent different concepts -- they are kept. "billing.payment_record" and "finance.payment" with ninety percent overlap are genuine duplicates -- one is consolidated.

The most complex check is graph topology. Depth-first cycle detection traverses the entire relationship web. Any remaining cycles are broken following the established hierarchy: convenience references first, structural relationships never, and no break may create a disconnected table. Siloed tables -- tables with no incoming or outgoing relationships -- are detected (Rule G09-R003) and either reconnected or removed.

The audit then zooms out to architecture. Product-domain location fit is examined: are tables in the right domain? Tables cannot cross division boundaries (Rule G06-R008). Eponymous tables -- like "inventory" in the "inventory" domain -- can never be relocated (Rule G06-R010). Child tables stay with their parent (Rule G06-R011). Domain balance is validated: operations and business domains must make up at least eighty percent of all domains.

Throughout, semantic distinction rules prevent false-positive duplicates. "payment_method" and "payment_channel" are not the same thing (Rule G02-SD01). "sla_target_time" and "sla_actual_time" answer different questions (Rule G02-SD03). "created_at" and "shipped_at" mark different milestones (Rule G02-SD04). A model that loses real business nuance in the name of tidiness is worse than one that tolerates a few extra columns.

After the quality checks, naming conventions are applied. Physical schema creation deploys the model to the Databricks Unity Catalog: databases in parallel, tables in parallel, foreign key constraints sequentially in dependency order. Data classification tags are applied to every column that needs them. And a static analysis examines the entire model, producing a confidence score and a set of forward-looking recommendations -- specific, actionable observations like "the billing domain has only four tables, consider adding charge_line_item" or "the operations domain has no connection to customer, consider adding customer_id on the work_order table."

Version zero -- the seed -- is complete. It is fully linked, fully tagged, physically deployed, and self-aware. It knows where it is strong, where it is weak, and what it recommends doing next.

These recommendations are the bridge to step two. The seed is planted. Now the modeler tends it.



================================================================================

STEP 2 -- VIBE MODELING

================================================================================


The seed model is version zero -- a solid, rule-compliant, fully deployed starting point. But it is still a starting point. It was built by a system that deeply understands the business, but has not yet heard from every stakeholder. The billing team has opinions. The operations team has requirements. The data governance committee has naming standards. None of them were in the room when version zero was born.

Vibe modeling is how their feedback reaches the model. The modeler writes natural language instructions -- plain English, not SQL, not JSON, not entity-relationship diagrams -- and the system translates those words into precise, validated changes. Each vibe creates a new version: v1, v2, v3, each one iterating on the seed, moving it closer to the ideal. No version is overwritten. The model evolves.



2.1 -- UNDERSTANDING INTENT: SURGICAL, HOLISTIC, AND GENERATIVE MODES


Before executing anything, the system must understand what the modeler truly wants. It reads the entire instruction in context and performs a five-dimensional analysis of intent: preservation (keep the model intact or rebuild?), scope (specific entities or broad principle?), change philosophy (targeted fix or new content?), satisfaction (polishing or frustrated?), and the core desired outcome.

This analysis produces one of three modes.


Surgical mode.

The modeler has identified specific problems with specific entities. Their implicit contract: "Fix these things. Leave everything else alone."

"Remove the duplicate status column from the invoice table." "Fix the FK from order to warehouse." "Drop the marketing domain."

In surgical mode, the system modifies exactly what was asked. No content is regenerated. No tables are rewritten. If the modeler asked to remove three columns and rename one, exactly four changes occur. The rest of the model is untouched.


Holistic mode.

The modeler wants a rule or pattern applied consistently across the entire model, without changing its structure. Their implicit contract: "Apply this principle everywhere, but preserve the structure."

"Ensure all primary keys follow the naming convention." "Add created_at and updated_at to every table." "Make sure every _id column is linked."

In holistic mode, the system touches many tables -- potentially all of them -- but formulaically. No creative decisions. No new tables unless explicitly requested. Just consistent application of a rule across the entire model.


Generative mode.

The modeler wants something fundamentally new. Their implicit contract: "Create or rebuild this part to be better."

"Add a loyalty domain with program, tier, reward, and enrollment." "This model feels too generic -- make it more industry-specific." "Split billing into invoicing and collections."

In generative mode, the modeler's instructions are decomposed into twelve tailored briefings -- one for each downstream worker (domain generation, product generation, attribute generation, in-domain linking, cross-domain linking, many-to-many detection, semantic deduplication, global deduplication, product merge, attribute deduplication, normalization, and cycle breaking). Each worker receives not a generic copy of the vibe, but a custom briefing with specific requirements, enriched guidance, explicit do's and don'ts, cautions about potential mistakes, and quality criteria. The result is creative without being chaotic: twelve workers, each interpreting the same vision through the lens of their own expertise.

After all changes -- whether surgical, holistic, or generative -- the model re-enters the same quality pipeline from step 1.8. The same rules apply. The same checks run. A table born during a vibe receives the same scrutiny as a table born during the original creation. Vibe modeling does not shortcut quality.



2.2 -- MODEL EVOLUTION: THE PROGRESSION TOWARD THE IDEAL


The real power of vibe modeling is not any single iteration. It is the progression.

Follow a model through four versions using a retail business as an example.

Version zero is the seed -- the base model from step one. Seven domains, forty-eight tables, five hundred columns, ninety-six foreign key relationships. The static analysis finds fifteen issues. Confidence score: seventy-two. The system's own recommendations say: fix three broken FK references in billing, link eight unlinked _id columns, connect the isolated workforce and procurement domains, and consider splitting the oversized product table.

Version one is the first vibe. The modeler takes the recommendations and adds their own twist: "Fix the broken FKs. Link all unlinked _id columns -- if the parent table doesn't exist, create it. Connect workforce to operations. Split product into catalog and pricing. Use retail jargon everywhere: sku not product_number, pos not point_of_sale." The system classifies this as generative, distributes the vibes to all twelve workers, and produces a refined model. Six issues remain. Confidence score: eighty-five.

Version two is the second vibe. The modeler writes: "Merge service_request and work_order into service_order with a discriminator. Connect procurement to product through supplier_product. Add a loyalty domain. Don't touch billing or logistics." Generative mode with explicit protection constraints. The merge executes, the loyalty domain is created and populated, procurement connects to product. Two minor issues remain. Confidence: ninety-three.

Version three is the third vibe. "Link loyalty.enrollment to customer.profile. Fix the naming in workforce." Surgical mode -- two targeted actions. Zero issues. Confidence: ninety-seven.

Four versions. Minutes, not months. Each built on the seed. Each was guided by the model's own self-assessment.

This progression is the key insight. The model is never "done" in the traditional sense. It is always moving toward the ideal -- the model that perfectly represents the business. Version one hundred is as valid as version two. Each version is a complete, self-contained snapshot. Each can be deployed independently. Each preserves the full history of what came before.

Every vibe creates a new version. No version is ever overwritten. The modeler never starts over. Every piece of work they have done is preserved. Every decision they have made is respected. The feedback loop is self-reinforcing: the static analysis produces recommendations, the modeler uses them (or ignores them), the next version improves, the next analysis refines further.

The cost of change is negligible. In a traditional model, renaming a domain is a week of work -- updating schemas, scripts, documentation, lineage. In vibe modeling, it is one sentence: "Rename the logistics domain to fulfillment." The cascade is automatic. The documentation regenerates. The physical schema updates. The foreign keys adjust. The tags reapply.

This is the philosophy of vibe modeling: plant the seed with step one, then tend it with step two. The seed is not a final answer -- it is a starting point. The first vibe is feedback from the billing team. The second is feedback from the operations team. The third is the naming standards review from governance. The fourth is the new requirement from the product team. Each iteration is fast, safe, guided, and precise. The result is a model that is not just technically correct, but organizationally owned.



================================================================================

THE ONTOLOGY AND DAG-FIRST MINDSET

================================================================================


There is a design philosophy that runs beneath every step described in this paper, and it deserves to be made explicit. The model is not built as a flat collection of tables. It is built as a graph -- specifically, a Directed Acyclic Graph -- and as an ontology. This dual nature is what makes it useful far beyond traditional SQL analytics.


The DAG-first mindset.

Every foreign key relationship in the model has a direction: from child to parent. "order.customer_id" points from order (child) to customer (parent). This directionality is not an implementation detail -- it is a semantic statement about how the business works. An order belongs to a customer; a customer does not belong to an order.

By enforcing the DAG constraint (Rule G09-R001) at every step -- during in-domain linking, during cross-domain linking, during quality checks, during vibe modeling -- the model guarantees three properties that matter in production.

First, insertion order is always deterministic. Parent tables are loaded before children. Foreign key constraints can be applied without conflict. ETL pipelines can be built by simply following the graph from roots to leaves.

Second, the model can be traversed without loops. Any query that follows foreign key paths will terminate. Any recursive traversal will converge. Any lineage trace will end at a root table. This is essential for data lineage tools, impact analysis, and automated documentation.

Third, the model is topologically sortable. The foreign key statements themselves are sorted in dependency order using Kahn's algorithm before physical deployment. This means the physical schema creation is deterministic and repeatable -- databases are created in parallel, tables are created in parallel, and foreign key constraints are applied sequentially in the exact order that guarantees every referenced table already exists.


The ontology mindset.

Alongside the physical schema, the model generates an RDFS (Resource Description Framework Schema) representation. This expresses every domain as a class, every table as a subclass, every attribute as a property with a defined range, and every foreign key as a semantic relationship. The result is not just a database schema -- it is a knowledge graph.

This matters because the same model can now serve multiple use cases without modification. SQL analysts query the physical tables in Unity Catalog. Data scientists ingest the machine-readable model definition. Knowledge graph platforms consume the RDFS ontology. Data governance tools read the tags and classifications. Lineage tools traverse the DAG. Each consumer sees the same model through a different lens, and each gets exactly what they need.

The ontology also makes the model discoverable. Semantic search can find tables by meaning, not just by name. "Show me everything related to customer payments" can traverse the ontology from the customer class through the billing relationship to the payment class -- even if the analyst does not know the exact table names.

This dual nature -- a physically deployed, governanced, DAG-structured relational schema and a semantically rich, traversable, class-based ontology -- is what separates a vibe data model from a spreadsheet of table definitions. The model is not just a plan for data storage. It is a machine-readable representation of how the business thinks about itself.



CLOSING


A data model is a claim. It claims: this is what this business is. These are its concepts. These are their relationships. This is where each piece of data belongs.

Vibe data modeling makes that claim in two steps. Step one plants the seed: it decomposes the organization into divisions, divisions into domains, domains into products, products into attributes -- then links, validates, deploys, and self-assesses. The output is version zero. Step two iterates on that seed through natural language -- understanding the modeler's intent, executing with precision, and validating with the same rigor as the original build. Each vibe produces a new version: v1, v2, v3, each closer to the ideal.

The result is a model that is not just technically correct, but organizationally owned. Every team has had their say. Every domain reflects the language its users actually speak. Every table exists because someone needed it.

The model is not a document filed away in a drawer. It is a living, versioned, evolvable structure -- deployed to a lakehouse, governed with classification tags, expressed as both a relational schema and a semantic ontology, and designed from birth to grow.

It is not perfect. No model ever is. But it is honest about its imperfections, and it tells its creator exactly where to look next.

G01 - NAMING CONVENTION RULES
================================================================================
How to name domains, tables, and columns consistently.

G01-R001: All names (domains, tables, columns) must use snake_case by default.
  Applies to: ALL
  Rationale: snake_case is the most readable convention for SQL-based platforms and avoids casing ambiguity across systems.
  Example: "customer_order_date" is correct. "CustomerOrderDate" or "customer-order-date" are not valid. If someone submits a table called "MyTable", it must be converted to "my_table".

G01-R002: Domain names must be exactly one word, lowercase, and no longer than 20 characters.
  Applies to: Domains
  Rationale: Short, single-word domain names are easier to reference in fully qualified paths like domain.table.column and keep SQL queries clean.
  Example: "customer", "sales", "logistics", "risk" are valid domain names. "customer_service" is invalid (two words), "CustomerData" is invalid (not lowercase), and "transportation_infrastructure" is invalid (exceeds 20 characters).

G01-R003: Domain names must be singular, never plural. Exception: naturally singular terms that happen to end in 's' like logistics, sales, or operations.
  Applies to: Domains
  Rationale: Singular names represent the concept itself (customer, not customers), aligning with standard data modeling conventions where the domain is a category, not a collection.
  Example: "customer" not "customers", "product" not "products", "employee" not "employees", "aircraft" not "aircrafts". Exception: "logistics" and "sales" are valid because they are singular discipline or department names, not plural forms.

G01-R004: Table names must be 1 to 3 words, lowercase, and no longer than 30 characters.
  Applies to: Products
  Rationale: Concise table names are easier to query, document, and discuss. Long names create friction in SQL and communication.
  Example: "profile" (1 word), "rate_schedule" (2 words), "order_line_item" (3 words) are all valid. "monthly_aggregated_customer_transaction_summary" violates both the word count and character limit.

G01-R005: Table names must NOT repeat the domain name as a prefix. In domain 'customer', the table should be 'account', NOT 'customer_account'.
  Applies to: Products
  Rationale: The domain already provides context. The full path customer.account is clear. customer.customer_account is redundant and verbose.
  Example: In the "sales" domain, the table should be "order" not "sales_order", "opportunity" not "sales_opportunity". In the "billing" domain, use "invoice" not "billing_invoice". The full path "sales.order" is already clear without repeating "sales".

G01-R006: Column names must NOT repeat the table name as a prefix, except for the primary key. In table 'customer', use 'status', NOT 'customer_status'.
  Applies to: Attributes
  Rationale: The table already provides context. customer.status is clear. customer.customer_status is redundant. The PK is the exception because it needs to be globally unique when referenced as a foreign key.
  Example: In the "account" table, use "balance" not "account_balance", "type" not "account_type". In the "order" table, use "date" not "order_date". The primary key "account_id" is the only exception — it keeps the table prefix because it must be recognizable when used as a foreign key in other tables.

G01-R007: Primary key names follow the pattern: table_name + suffix (for example, customer_id, order_id).
  Applies to: Attributes
  Rationale: Consistent PK naming makes foreign key relationships self-documenting. When you see customer_id in any table, you immediately know it references the customer table.
  Example: The "customer" table has "customer_id", the "order" table has "order_id", the "profile" table has "profile_id", the "loan" table has "loan_id". Seeing "profile_id" anywhere in the model instantly tells you it comes from the profile table.

G01-R008: Foreign key column names must END WITH the target table's primary key name. Descriptive prefixes are allowed (for example, billing_address_id and shipping_address_id both reference address.address_id).
  Applies to: Attributes
  Rationale: The ends-with convention makes FK relationships discoverable by inspection. The prefix conveys the business role of the relationship while the suffix identifies the target.
  Example: "driver_employee_id" ends with "employee_id" — it links to the employee table. "billing_address_id" ends with "address_id" — it links to the address table. "source_warehouse_id" ends with "warehouse_id" — it links to the warehouse table. The prefix describes the business role; the suffix identifies the target.

G01-R009: Column names must not exceed 50 characters.
  Applies to: Attributes
  Rationale: Excessively long names hurt readability in queries, reports, and documentation. If a name needs more than 50 characters, it likely needs simplification.
  Example: "temperature_ramp_rate_c_per_min" (30 characters) is well within the limit. "average_monthly_customer_transaction_amount_in_local_currency_usd" (65 characters) exceeds the limit and should be simplified to something like "avg_monthly_txn_amount_usd".

G01-R010: All names must be lowercase, contain only letters, numbers, and underscores, and must not start with a digit.
  Applies to: ALL
  Rationale: This ensures names are valid SQL identifiers across all platforms without needing quoting or escaping.
  Example: "order_date", "address_line_1", "rate_v2" are all valid. "Order_Date" (contains uppercase), "3rd_party_id" (starts with a digit), and "customer-name" (contains a hyphen) are all invalid.

G01-R011: Association (junction) table names must NOT repeat the domain name as a prefix, same as regular tables.
  Applies to: Products
  Rationale: Consistency matters. The same naming rule applies regardless of whether the table is a regular entity or a many-to-many junction.
  Example: In the "education" domain, a junction table linking students and courses should be named "enrollment", not "education_enrollment". The path "education.enrollment" already provides domain context. Use semantically meaningful names like "enrollment", "assignment", or "authorship" — never generic names like "link", "mapping", or "junction".

G01-R012: When two duplicate tables are merged into a shared location, use the most generic, domain-agnostic name (for example, shared.contact instead of customer.contact_info).
  Applies to: Products
  Rationale: A shared table serves multiple domains, so its name should not favor any single domain's terminology.
  Example: When merging "sales.interaction" and "support.interaction", the merged table becomes "shared.interaction" — not "shared.sales_interaction". When merging "customer.contact" and "vendor.contact" (both represent contact information), the merged name should be "shared.contact" — a generic, neutral term that does not favor either domain. NOTE: Examples are illustrative — use terminology appropriate to the actual industry being modeled.

G01-R013: Column names must be clear, semantic, and human-readable. Prioritize clarity over brevity.
  Applies to: Attributes
  Rationale: A business user should understand what a column contains from its name alone, without needing to look up documentation.
  Example: Use "mean_time_between_failures" instead of "mtbf". Use "energy_consumption_mwh" instead of "ec". Use "feed_rate_kg_per_min" instead of "fr_kpm". A business user seeing "mean_time_between_failures" understands it instantly; "mtbf" requires a lookup.

G01-R014: Preserve important qualifiers in column names: units (kg, mwh, percent), rates (per_min, per_hour), and standard terms (mean_time_between_failures).
  Applies to: Attributes
  Rationale: Stripping unit qualifiers creates ambiguity. Is 'temperature' in Celsius or Fahrenheit? Is 'rate' per minute or per hour? The qualifier answers these questions.
  Example: Use "temperature_ramp_rate_c_per_min" not just "temperature_rate". Use "energy_consumption_mwh" not just "energy_consumption". Use "feed_rate_kg_per_min" not just "feed_rate". The qualifiers "_c_per_min", "_mwh", and "_kg_per_min" tell you exactly what unit and rate the measurement is in.

G01-R015: Use industry jargon in names only when it is universally recognized in the industry. Otherwise prefer the full term over the abbreviation.
  Applies to: ALL
  Rationale: Industry-standard abbreviations are fine when every professional in that industry knows them. But lazy abbreviations like 'cust_nm' are never clearer than 'customer_name' in any industry.
  Example: In any industry, use standard abbreviations that every professional recognizes (e.g., "sku" in retail, "icd_code" in healthcare, "iban" in banking). But abbreviations like "cust_nm" for "customer_name" or "prod_cat" for "product_category" are not acceptable — always use the full term when the abbreviation is not a recognized industry standard.

G01-R016: Compound business terms are allowed as table names when removing the first word would make the name ambiguous. For example, order_item, payment_method, and customer_segment are valid compound names.
  Applies to: Products
  Rationale: 'item' alone is too generic and could mean anything. 'order_item' is a recognized business concept. The test: if removing the first word loses meaning, keep it.
  Example: "order_item" is valid because "item" alone is too generic. "payment_method" is valid because "method" alone is meaningless. "customer_segment" is valid because "segment" alone is ambiguous. But "customer_account" inside the "customer" domain is NOT valid — "account" is clear enough on its own, so the compound duplicates the domain prefix.

G01-R022: When a table is moved to a different domain, keep its original name. Do not rename tables during relocation.
  Applies to: Products
  Rationale: Renaming during relocation introduces unnecessary breaking changes to queries and documentation that reference the table.
  Example: If the "order" table is moved from the "sales" domain to the "commerce" domain, it stays named "order" — it does not get renamed to "commerce_request" or "sales_transaction". The table keeps its identity; only its domain address changes from "sales.order" to "commerce.order".

G01-R023: Association (junction) table names must be semantically meaningful business terms. Generic-only names like link, mapping, bridge, junction, association, and relationship are forbidden.
  Applies to: Products
  Rationale: Generic names convey zero business meaning. A junction table represents a real business concept — the name should reflect what that concept is, not just that a link exists.
  Example: A table linking students to courses should be called "enrollment" — not "student_course_link" or "mapping". A table linking employees to projects should be "assignment" — not "bridge" or "junction". A table linking products to suppliers should be "supply_agreement" — not "relationship". If the only name you can think of is "link" or "mapping", the M:N relationship probably should not exist.

G01-R024: Product (table) names must NOT end with analytics-oriented suffixes: _analysis, _analytics, _report, _summary, _aggregate, _dashboard, _metrics, _kpi, _score, _model, or _prediction.
  Applies to: Products
  Rationale: These suffixes indicate derived, computed, or analytics-layer artifacts that do not belong in the operational data model. The model stores raw business facts, not derived insights.
  Example: "fraud_detection", "churn_prediction", "revenue_analysis", "sales_summary", "performance_metrics", "usage_statistics", and "cdr_analysis" are all forbidden. If a table name ends with any of these suffixes, it belongs in the analytics layer, not the business data model. Store the raw events (transactions, interactions, usage records) instead.

G01-R025: A domain prefix in a product name is justified ONLY when another domain already has a table with the same base name, requiring disambiguation. Otherwise, the domain prefix is unnecessary redundancy.
  Applies to: Products
  Rationale: G01-R005 forbids repeating the domain as a prefix. The only legitimate exception is when a base name collision exists across domains and the prefix is needed for disambiguation.
  Example: If the "sales" domain has a table "order" and the "procurement" domain also needs a table named "order", then "sales_order" and "purchase_order" are justified — the prefix disambiguates. But if no other domain has a table named "emission", then "carbon.carbon_emission" is unjustified — rename it to "carbon.emission" since no collision exists.

G01-R026: The physical table name must exactly match the product (logical) name in snake_case. The physical column name must exactly match the attribute (logical) name. No divergence between logical and physical names is allowed.
  Applies to: Products, Attributes
  Rationale: Divergence between logical and physical names creates confusion — documentation says one name, queries use another. Keeping them identical ensures one source of truth for naming.
  Example: If the logical product name is "rate_schedule", the physical table name must also be "rate_schedule" — not "tbl_rate_schedule", "RATE_SCHEDULE", or "RateSchedule". If the logical attribute name is "start_date", the physical column name must also be "start_date" — not "START_DATE", "startDate", or "dt_start".

G02 - SEMANTIC DEDUPLICATION RULES
================================================================================
How to detect and resolve duplicate concepts to maintain a Single Source of Truth.

G02-R001: Each business concept must have ONE and ONLY ONE authoritative table across the entire model. This is the Single Source of Truth (SSOT) principle.
  Applies to: Products
  Rationale: If 'customer' data lives in two tables, every downstream consumer must decide which one to trust. SSOT eliminates this confusion and ensures consistent reporting.
  Example: If both the "customer" domain and the "client" domain have a table storing people's names, emails, and phone numbers, only one can be the authoritative source. The other domain must reference it via foreign key. A business user asking "who is this person?" should never have to choose between two competing tables.

G02-R002: Duplicate detection must be conservative. Only flag tables as duplicates when they clearly represent the same business concept with high confidence.
  Applies to: Products
  Rationale: Falsely merging two tables that serve different purposes destroys business data. It is safer to keep a possible duplicate than to merge incorrectly.
  Example: Two tables with 70% similar columns should NOT automatically be flagged as duplicates. A "customer_complaint" table and a "customer_feedback" table may look similar but serve different business purposes — complaints track issues for resolution while feedback captures satisfaction for product improvement. Only flag duplicates when confidence exceeds 80%.

G02-R003: The same customer/person concept in multiple domains is a duplicate that must be resolved. For example, customer.profile and client.profile both represent the same person and should not coexist.
  Applies to: Products
  Rationale: A business user asking 'who is this customer?' should never have to decide between two tables that both claim to own customer identity.
  Example: If the "customer" domain has a "profile" table with name, email, and phone, and the "client" domain also has a "profile" table with the same person data, these are duplicates. Whether you call them "customer", "client", "member", "patient", "policyholder", or "guest", they all represent the same thing — the person/entity the business serves. Designate one as authoritative and have all others reference it.

G02-R004: Industry synonyms for the same concept are duplicates. For example, invoice and bill mean the same thing; usage and consumption mean the same thing; customer and client mean the same thing.
  Applies to: Products, Attributes
  Rationale: Different names do not justify separate tables when the underlying business concept is identical. Pick one canonical term and use it consistently.
  Example: A "billing.invoice" table and a "finance.bill" table represent the same concept — a charge sent to a customer. Similarly, "logistics.shipment" and "delivery.consignment" both track movement of goods. Column-level synonyms also apply: "termination_date" and "end_date" mean the same thing, as do "creation_date" and "created_at". Pick one canonical name and use it everywhere.

G02-R005: Master data and transactional data are NEVER duplicates, even if they share similar names. For example, customer profile (master) and customer subscription (transactional) serve completely different purposes.
  Applies to: Products
  Rationale: Master data describes what an entity IS. Transactional data describes what HAPPENED. These are fundamentally different categories that answer different questions.
  Example: "customer.profile" (master data describing who the customer is) and "customer.subscription" (transactional data describing what the customer bought) are fundamentally different. Similarly, "product_catalog" (master — what is available) and "order" (transactional — what was purchased) are not duplicates. Master data changes slowly; transactional data grows continuously.

G02-R006: Different lifecycle stages are NOT duplicates. For example, a raw data record and its processed version, or a request and its confirmation, represent different states of the same process.
  Applies to: Products
  Rationale: Each stage captures a distinct snapshot in time and may have different attributes, timestamps, and business rules. They answer different questions.
  Example: A "quote" table (a proposed deal) and an "order" table (a confirmed deal) represent different stages of the sales process. A "raw_usage" table (unprocessed metering data) and "rated_usage" (priced metering data) capture different processing stages. A "draft_catalog" and "published_catalog" are different lifecycle states. Keep all of them.

G02-R007: Different granularity levels are NOT duplicates. For example, individual usage events and aggregated usage summaries serve different analytical purposes.
  Applies to: Products
  Rationale: A detail record and its summary exist at different grains and serve different audiences. Analysts need both for drill-down and roll-up.
  Example: A "usage_event" table storing every individual call or data session (one row per event) and an "aggregated_usage" table summarizing monthly totals (one row per customer per month) exist at different granularity levels. Analysts need the detail records for drill-down investigation and the summaries for dashboard reporting. These are not duplicates.

G02-R008: Parent and child entities are structurally different and must both be kept. For example, order (parent) and order_item (child) are not duplicates.
  Applies to: Products
  Rationale: Parent-child relationships represent a hierarchy, not duplication. An order contains items; removing either destroys the relationship.
  Example: An "order" table (parent) contains header information like date, customer, and total amount. An "order_item" table (child) contains line-level details like product, quantity, and unit price. They are not duplicates — the order groups the items, and the items detail the order. Similarly, "purchase_order" (parent) and "purchase_order_line" (child) must both exist.

G02-R009: When two tables from different domains overlap significantly (60% or more of their purpose), prefer merging them into a shared location over deleting one.
  Applies to: Products, Domains
  Rationale: Merging preserves all data and attributes from both sources. Deletion risks losing unique attributes that only one version had.
  Example: If "customer.interaction" and "support.interaction" share 85% of the same columns and purpose, merge them into "shared.interaction" rather than deleting one. The merged table inherits all unique columns from both sources, so nothing is lost. Deleting one would risk losing the columns that were unique to it.

G02-R010: When merging overlapping tables, add a discriminator column to preserve the original domain context (for example, interaction_source_type with values 'sales' or 'support').
  Applies to: Products, Attributes
  Rationale: After merging, users need to know where each row originated. The discriminator column prevents information loss and enables filtering.
  Example: After merging "sales.interaction" and "support.interaction" into "shared.interaction", add a column "interaction_source_type" with values 'sales' or 'support'. After merging "customer.contact" and "vendor.contact" into "shared.contact", add "contact_type" with values 'customer' or 'vendor'. This lets users filter: "show me only support interactions" or "show me only vendor contacts". NOTE: Examples are illustrative — use terminology appropriate to the actual industry.

G02-R011: Only remove a table as a duplicate if the overlap with another table is 85% or higher.
  Applies to: Products
  Rationale: Below 85% overlap, the tables likely serve sufficiently different purposes to justify their separate existence.
  Example: If "billing.payment_record" and "finance.payment" overlap by 75%, do NOT remove either — the 25% difference likely represents important domain-specific attributes. Only at 85% or higher overlap (like "billing.invoice" and "accounting.bill" sharing 90% of their columns and purpose) is removal justified.

G02-R012: Only merge two domains if their functional overlap exceeds 60%.
  Applies to: Domains
  Rationale: Below 60%, the domains represent meaningfully different business capabilities, even if they share some concepts.
  Example: If two proposed domains "customer_service" and "customer_support" share 65% of the same tables and concepts, they should be merged into a single "customer" domain. But if "sales" and "marketing" only share 40% of their concepts, they represent distinct business functions and should remain separate domains.

G02-R013: Core business tables that are fundamental to operations must NEVER be merged into a shared domain or removed.
  Applies to: Products, Domains
  Rationale: Core tables like customer, invoice, or product are the backbone of the model. Merging them away creates confusion about where the authoritative data lives.
  Example: The "customer.profile" table, "billing.invoice" table, and "sales.order" table are fundamental to business operations. Even if a similar "shared.contact" table exists, you cannot merge "customer.profile" into it and delete the original. Core tables stay in their authoritative domain — they are the identity of the business model.

G02-R014: If one table is generic (like shared.contact) and another is domain-specific (like customer.profile), they are NOT duplicates. Keep both.
  Applies to: Products
  Rationale: The generic version serves cross-domain needs while the domain-specific version serves specialized requirements. They complement each other.
  Example: "shared.contact" is a generic table that stores contact information for all entity types — vendors, partners, employees, and customers. "customer.profile" is a domain-specific table with customer-only attributes like loyalty tier and marketing preferences. They are NOT duplicates. Similarly, "shared.location" (generic coordinates) and "operations.facility" (facility-specific attributes) complement each other. NOTE: Examples are illustrative — use terminology appropriate to the actual industry.

G02-R015: Tables with the same name in different domains but representing different business concepts are NOT duplicates. For example, customer.device (equipment the customer owns) and operations.device (operational equipment) are completely different concepts.
  Applies to: Products
  Rationale: Names can collide across domains. The business meaning, not the name, determines whether something is a duplicate.
  Example: "customer.device" (equipment the customer purchased) and "operations.device" (operational equipment managed by the business) both use the name "device" but represent entirely different business concepts with different attributes. The customer device has purchase date and warranty; the operational device has maintenance schedule and location. Name collision does not equal duplication.

G02-R016: When checking for duplicate columns within a table, only flag them with 80% or higher confidence. False positives (removing valid columns) are worse than false negatives (keeping possible duplicates).
  Applies to: Attributes
  Rationale: Removing a column that turns out to be essential is a destructive operation. Keeping a slightly redundant column is merely wasteful. Err on the side of keeping.
  Example: In a table with both "termination_date" and "end_date", these are likely duplicates at 90%+ confidence — flag them. But "start_date" and "effective_date" might have subtle business differences (a contract might start on one date but become effective on another) — if confidence is only 60%, do NOT flag them as duplicates.

G02-R017: No two domains may have the same name.
  Applies to: Domains
  Rationale: Domain names are unique identifiers. Duplicates would break every reference path and create ambiguity.
  Example: You cannot have two domains both named "finance". If one handles accounting and the other handles investment management, they need distinct names like "accounting" and "investment". Duplicate domain names would make paths like "finance.ledger" ambiguous — which finance domain does it belong to?

G02-R018: No two tables within the same domain may have the same name.
  Applies to: Products
  Rationale: Table names must be unique within their domain to form unambiguous domain.table reference paths.
  Example: Within the "customer" domain, you cannot have two tables both named "profile". If one stores personal details and the other stores preferences, name them "profile" and "preference". The path "customer.profile" must point to exactly one table.

G02-R019: No two columns within the same table may have the same name.
  Applies to: Attributes
  Rationale: Duplicate column names within a table are invalid in SQL and create ambiguity for every consumer.
  Example: A table cannot have two columns both named "status". If one tracks order fulfillment status and the other tracks payment status, they must have distinct names — in this case, they likely belong in their respective tables (order and payment) rather than being duplicated in one place.

G02-R020: Primary key and foreign key attributes are protected from removal during attribute deduplication. They must never be flagged or removed as duplicates, regardless of name similarity.
  Applies to: Attributes
  Rationale: Removing a PK destroys the table's identity. Removing an FK destroys a relationship. These are structural columns that anchor the model's integrity and must be exempt from any dedup process.
  Example: If a table has both "customer_id" (FK to customer) and "client_id" (a business attribute), only "client_id" can be considered for dedup — "customer_id" is a protected FK. If a table has "order_id" (PK) and another column that looks similar, the PK is never a dedup candidate. Even if "employee_id" (FK) and "staff_id" (business attribute) look redundant, the FK is protected and only the business attribute can be evaluated.

G02-R021: When tables with the same name exist in different domains, consolidate them into the primary domain if their attribute overlap is 70% or higher. If overlap is below 70%, keep both but rename the secondary with a domain prefix for disambiguation.
  Applies to: Products, Domains
  Rationale: Same-named tables across domains with high overlap are true duplicates that violate SSOT. But when overlap is low, the tables represent genuinely different concepts that happen to share a name — renaming disambiguates without losing data.
  Example: If "sales.order" and "procurement.order" share 75% of their attributes, consolidate into one — they represent the same concept. But if "customer.device" and "iot.device" share only 30% of attributes, keep both and rename the secondary to "iot.iot_device" to disambiguate. The 70% threshold separates genuine duplicates from name collisions.


================================================================================
G03 - FOREIGN KEY RULES
================================================================================
How to create and maintain foreign key relationships.

G03-R001: A foreign key must always reference the actual primary key column of the target table, not some other column.
  Applies to: Relations
  Rationale: Foreign keys are defined as references to primary keys. Pointing to a non-PK column breaks referential integrity and confuses the relationship semantics.
  Example: If the "order" table has a foreign key to the "customer" table, it must reference "customer_id" (the actual primary key), not "customer_email" or "customer_code" — even if those columns happen to be unique. If a FK accidentally points to the wrong column, it must be corrected to point to the real PK.

G03-R002: NEVER create a self-referencing foreign key (a table pointing back to itself) UNLESS the column name starts with a recognized hierarchical prefix: parent_, manager_, reporting_, supervisor_, alternate_, original_, superseded_, duplicate_, follow_up_, or ultimate_parent_. All other self-referencing FKs are blocked.
  Applies to: Relations
  Rationale: Self-referencing FKs create structural ambiguity and complicate queries. The only legitimate exception is hierarchical relationships (org charts, part assemblies, category trees) where the prefix explicitly declares the hierarchy type, preventing ambiguity.
  Example: "employee.manager_employee_id" referencing the same "employee" table is allowed because it starts with "manager_" — it represents a reporting hierarchy. "category.parent_category_id" is allowed because it starts with "parent_" — it represents a category tree. But a generic "employee.employee_id" referencing itself without a hierarchical prefix is blocked. If you need a hierarchy but cannot use one of the recognized prefixes, create a separate hierarchy table instead.

G03-R003: NEVER create bidirectional foreign keys between two tables (table A references B AND table B references A). This creates a cycle.
  Applies to: Relations
  Rationale: Bidirectional FKs make it impossible to determine which table to load first and create circular dependencies that break insert/delete operations.
  Example: If the "order" table has "customer_id" pointing to the "customer" table, then the "customer" table must NOT have a "latest_order_id" pointing back to the "order" table. This would create a chicken-and-egg problem: you cannot insert a customer without an order, and you cannot insert an order without a customer.

G03-R004: The data type of a foreign key column must match the data type of the primary key it references.
  Applies to: Relations, Attributes
  Rationale: Type mismatches between FK and PK make joins fail or produce unexpected results. If the PK is BIGINT, the FK must also be BIGINT.
  Example: If the "customer" table has "customer_id" of type BIGINT, then every foreign key referencing it (like "order.customer_id") must also be BIGINT. A STRING foreign key trying to join to a BIGINT primary key will either fail or produce incorrect results depending on the database platform. Numeric types (BIGINT, INT) are compatible with each other; string types (STRING, VARCHAR) are compatible with each other; but mixing across groups is not allowed.

G03-R005: Every foreign key must point to a table that actually exists in the model. Dangling references to non-existent tables are invalid.
  Applies to: Relations
  Rationale: A FK to a table that does not exist is broken by definition. It provides no referential integrity and confuses anyone reading the model.
  Example: If the "order" table has "warehouse_id" claiming to reference "warehouse.warehouse_id", but no "warehouse" table exists anywhere in the model, this is a dangling reference. It must be fixed by either creating the missing warehouse table or removing the foreign key annotation from the column.

G03-R007: System identifier columns (ending in _reference, _ref, _code, _number) are NOT foreign key candidates. They are reference numbers or codes, not relationships to other tables.
  Applies to: Attributes
  Rationale: Not every column ending in _id or a similar suffix is a foreign key. Columns like external_reference_id, batch_number, or legacy_code are technical identifiers, not business relationships.
  Example: Columns like "external_reference_id", "batch_number", "country_code", "currency_code", "legacy_ref", and "source_system" are business identifiers or system references — not foreign keys to other tables. Even though "country_code" ends with "_code", it is a standard ISO value, not a relationship to a "country" table. Do not create FK relationships for these columns.

G03-R008: When a foreign key column name could match tables in multiple domains, prefer the table in the 'shared' domain as the target.
  Applies to: Relations
  Rationale: Shared domain tables exist precisely to serve as cross-domain references. They are the natural target when ambiguity exists.
  Example: If "order.address_id" could reference either "customer.address" or "shared.address", prefer "shared.address" as the target because shared-domain tables are designed to serve as cross-domain reference points. The shared domain exists precisely for these ambiguous cases where multiple domains have similar tables.

G03-R010: When a new foreign key relationship is added, identify and remove columns that are now redundant because the same data is accessible through the FK join.
  Applies to: Relations, Attributes
  Rationale: If you add customer_id to the order table, you no longer need customer_name, customer_email in the order table. Those values should be accessed by joining to the customer table. This enforces normalization.
  Example: After adding "customer_id" as a foreign key on the "order" table, remove "customer_name" and "customer_email" from the order table — those values are now accessible by joining to the customer table. Similarly, after adding "address_id" to an account table, remove embedded address fields like "street", "city", "state", and "zip_code".

G03-R014: Role-qualified foreign keys are valid. Multiple FK columns can reference the same target table using descriptive prefixes. For example, billing_address_id and shipping_address_id both correctly reference the address table.
  Applies to: Relations
  Rationale: Many business relationships involve the same entity type in different roles. An order has a billing address AND a shipping address. Both are valid FKs to the same address table.
  Example: An "order" table can have both "billing_address_id" and "shipping_address_id", both referencing the same "address" table. A "trip" table can have "source_warehouse_id" and "destination_warehouse_id", both referencing the "warehouse" table. A "project" table can have "hiring_manager_id" and "reporting_manager_id", both referencing the "employee" table. Each FK plays a distinct business role.

G03-R015: Parent-child foreign keys (child referencing its parent) must NEVER be removed during model optimization or cycle breaking.
  Applies to: Relations
  Rationale: The parent-child FK is the structural backbone of a hierarchy. Removing it destroys the fundamental relationship the child depends on.
  Example: The "order_item.order_id" foreign key linking each line item to its parent order must never be removed, even if it participates in a cycle. Similarly, "purchase_order_line.purchase_order_id" linking a PO line to its parent PO is sacred. These parent-child links are the structural backbone — breaking them would make the child records orphans with no connection to their parent.

G03-R018: If a table has two or more foreign key columns pointing to the same target table without a clear business reason (like role qualifiers), keep only one.
  Applies to: Relations
  Rationale: Duplicate FKs to the same target without distinct business roles (billing vs shipping) indicate a modeling error. One FK is sufficient.
  Example: If a table has both "customer_id" and "client_id" pointing to the same "customer" table with no distinct business roles, this is a duplicate — keep only one. But if a table has "billing_address_id" and "shipping_address_id" both pointing to the "address" table, keep both because they serve different business roles (where to bill vs where to ship).

G03-R020: FK reference values must use the fully qualified path format: domain.product.column (e.g., customer.profile.profile_id). At minimum, domain.product is required.
  Applies to: Relations
  Rationale: Fully qualified FK references eliminate ambiguity about which domain and table is being referenced. A bare column name or product name is ambiguous when multiple domains have similar tables.
  Example: "customer.profile.profile_id" is a properly formatted FK reference — it specifies the domain (customer), the product (profile), and the column (profile_id). "profile.profile_id" without a domain is ambiguous if multiple domains have a "profile" table. "profile_id" alone is completely unresolvable.

G03-R021: FK reference values containing placeholder or garbage text are invalid and must be cleared. Invalid values include: "unknown", "none", "null", "n/a", "tbd", or any value containing angle brackets (< or >).
  Applies to: Relations
  Rationale: A FK reference to "unknown" or "tbd" provides no structural information and misleads consumers into thinking a relationship exists when it does not. Clearing invalid references makes the model honest about what is linked and what is not.
  Example: An attribute with foreign_key_to = "unknown.table.column_id" is invalid — clear it. "none" as a FK target is invalid — clear it. "<pending_resolution>" is invalid — clear it. "tbd" is invalid — clear it. After clearing, the attribute remains in the table but has no FK annotation, signaling that the relationship has not been established rather than faking one with placeholder text.

G04 - PRIMARY KEY RULES
================================================================================
How to define and manage primary keys.

G04-R001: Every table MUST have a primary key. No exceptions.
  Applies to: Products, Attributes
  Rationale: A primary key uniquely identifies each row. Without it, you cannot reliably reference, update, or delete specific records.
  Example: A "payment" table must have "payment_id" as its primary key. An "enrollment" junction table must have "enrollment_id". Even small reference tables like "country" must have "country_id". A table without a primary key cannot guarantee row uniqueness and cannot be reliably referenced by foreign keys from other tables.

G04-R002: When no primary key is explicitly defined, default to the pattern table_name + _id (for example, customer_id for the customer table).
  Applies to: Attributes
  Rationale: Consistent PK naming makes the model self-documenting. Anyone seeing customer_id anywhere in the model knows it comes from the customer table.
  Example: The "customer" table automatically gets "customer_id", the "order" table gets "order_id", the "invoice" table gets "invoice_id", and the "rate_schedule" table gets "rate_schedule_id". If someone creates a table and forgets to define a PK, this pattern is applied automatically so the model stays consistent.

G04-R003: Primary keys should use a numeric type (BIGINT by default) unless there is a specific business reason for a different type.
  Applies to: Attributes
  Rationale: Numeric keys are compact, fast to index, and efficient for joins. String PKs are acceptable when business identifiers are naturally strings (like UUID).
  Example: "customer_id BIGINT", "order_id BIGINT", "payment_id BIGINT" are the standard. Numeric types are compact (8 bytes), fast to compare, and efficient for indexing and joining. Use STRING type only when the business requires it — for instance, if the company uses UUIDs as identifiers or if the primary key is a natural business code like an ISO country code.

G04-R005: A table's own primary key is never treated as an orphaned foreign key, even though it ends with the FK suffix pattern.
  Applies to: Attributes
  Rationale: The PK belongs to its own table. customer.customer_id is the identity of the customer table, not a reference to another table.
  Example: The column "employee_id" in the "employee" table ends with "_id" and looks like it could be a foreign key to an "employee" table — but it IS the employee table's own primary key. It should be skipped during orphaned FK detection, not linked to itself. Similarly, "order_id" in the "order" table is the order's identity, not a reference to some other table.

G04-R006: When two tables are merged, only one primary key survives. The merged table uses a single PK.
  Applies to: Products, Attributes
  Rationale: A merged table is one table and can only have one primary key. The old PKs from both sources are consolidated.
  Example: If "customer.interaction" (with "interaction_id") and "support.interaction" (with "interaction_id") are merged into "shared.interaction", the merged table has a single "interaction_id" primary key. The old PKs from both source tables are replaced by this one. You cannot have two primary keys in one table.

G04-R007: When a table is split into multiple tables, each new table gets its own primary key following the standard naming pattern.
  Applies to: Products, Attributes
  Rationale: Each table must be independently addressable. A split creates new entities, each needing their own identity.
  Example: If a bloated "customer" table is split into "profile" and "preference", the new tables get "profile_id" and "preference_id" respectively — each following the standard table_name + _id pattern. The original "customer_id" does not carry over as-is to either table; each new entity gets its own properly named primary key.

G04-R009: Association (junction) tables follow the same PK naming convention: the association table name + _id (for example, enrollment_id for the enrollment junction table).
  Applies to: Attributes
  Rationale: Consistency. Even junction tables are full entities that need a proper primary key for referencing.
  Example: A junction table called "enrollment" linking students to courses gets "enrollment_id" as its PK. A junction table called "assignment" linking employees to projects gets "assignment_id". A junction table called "authorship" linking books to authors gets "authorship_id". The PK is always the table's own semantic name + _id, not a composite of the two related table names.

G04-R010: A primary key name is invalid if it is: empty, the bare suffix alone (just "_id"), starts with an underscore, equals the generic word "id", or does not end with the configured PK suffix. The only valid pattern is table_name + suffix (e.g., customer_id).
  Applies to: Attributes
  Rationale: Invalid PK names break the self-documenting convention that lets anyone identify which table a PK belongs to. A bare "id" is ambiguous across every table; a leading underscore is a malformed identifier.
  Example: For a "customer" table: "customer_id" is valid. "_customer_id" is invalid (leading underscore). "id" is invalid (generic, no table context). "_id" is invalid (bare suffix, no table name). "customer_code" is invalid (does not end with _id suffix). "cust" is invalid (no suffix at all). Each of these must be corrected to "customer_id" following the table_name + _id pattern.

G05 - NORMALIZATION RULES
================================================================================
How to enforce Third Normal Form (3NF) and handle denormalization.

G05-R001: Only flag a column as denormalized when you are highly confident (95%+) that it is a copy of data that belongs in another table and should be accessed through a foreign key join instead.
  Applies to: Attributes
  Rationale: Removing a column that is actually essential destroys data. The 95% threshold ensures only clear-cut violations are flagged.
  Example: "customer_name" on the "order" table is clearly a copy of the name from the "customer" table when a "customer_id" FK already exists — flag it at 98% confidence. But "description" on the "order" table might seem similar to "description" on the "product" table, yet they describe different things (order notes vs product specs) — do not flag it at only 60% confidence.

G05-R002: Columns that describe properties of the table itself are NEVER denormalized, even if they share a name with columns in other tables. For example, warehouse.capacity is a property of the warehouse, not a copy from somewhere else.
  Applies to: Attributes
  Rationale: Every table has its own attributes. Just because two tables both have a 'name' column does not mean one is copied from the other.
  Example: "capacity" on the "warehouse" table is the warehouse's own capacity — it is NOT a copy from some other table. "square_footage" on the "facility" table is the facility's own measurement. Even if a "warehouse" table and a "store" table both have a "capacity" column, they independently own that property. Self-attributes are never denormalized.

G05-R003: Measurement and property columns (depth, weight, volume, grade, rate, amount, cost, price, quantity, area, length, height, temperature, pressure) always belong to their entity.
  Applies to: Attributes
  Rationale: Measurements are intrinsic to the thing being measured. A stockpile's tonnage is not denormalized from a different table; it IS the stockpile's data.
  Example: "quantity" on the "order_item" table is the item's own quantity ordered — keep it. "tonnage" on the "stockpile" table is the stockpile's own weight measurement. "temperature" on the "reactor" table is the reactor's current reading. "cost" on the "purchase_order" table is the PO's total cost. These measurements are intrinsic to their entity and must never be removed as denormalization.

G05-R004: When in doubt about whether a column is denormalized, do NOT remove it. Keeping a slightly redundant column is far less harmful than removing a column that turns out to be essential.
  Applies to: Attributes
  Rationale: Data loss is irreversible. Redundancy is merely wasteful. Always err on the side of preservation.
  Example: If you are unsure whether "region_name" on the "sales_territory" table is a copy from a "region" table or a property of the territory itself, keep it. Wrongly removing it would destroy data that may be the only place this information is stored. A slightly denormalized model still works; a model with missing essential columns is broken.

G05-R005: If a column has a legitimate business reason to exist in a table despite technically violating 3NF (for reporting performance, audit trail, or operational convenience), keep it.
  Applies to: Attributes
  Rationale: Pure 3NF is a theoretical ideal. In practice, some strategic denormalization improves query performance and simplifies common access patterns.
  Example: Storing "customer_name" on an "invoice" table technically violates 3NF because the name lives in the "customer" table. But if the business needs to preserve the customer name at the time of invoicing (since names can change later) for audit trail purposes, this is a legitimate business reason to keep it. The column serves as a historical snapshot, not just a redundant copy.

G05-R006: Columns that end with the PK suffix (like _id) but have no foreign key relationship defined are orphaned FKs that should be linked to their target table.
  Applies to: Relations, Attributes
  Rationale: An unlinked _id column usually represents a relationship that was intended but never formalized. Linking it completes the model's referential integrity.
  Example: If the "order" table has a column "warehouse_id" with no foreign key annotation, but a "warehouse" table exists in the model, this is an orphaned FK. It should be formally linked: order.warehouse_id references warehouse.warehouse_id. The relationship was clearly intended (the naming makes it obvious) but was never formalized. Linking it completes the model.

G05-R007: When fixing denormalization, always add the foreign key relationship FIRST, then remove the redundant columns SECOND.
  Applies to: Relations, Attributes
  Rationale: If you remove columns before establishing the FK, you lose data with no way to recover it. Adding the FK first ensures the join path exists before you clean up.
  Example: If the "order" table has "customer_name" and "customer_email" but no "customer_id" FK, do NOT delete the name and email first. Step 1: Add "customer_id" as a foreign key to the "customer" table. Step 2: Only after the FK link is in place, remove "customer_name" and "customer_email" since they can now be accessed through the join. Reversing this order would lose the customer reference entirely.

G05-R008: Address columns (street, city, state, zip) are denormalized ONLY if the table is NOT an address or location table AND a separate address table exists in the model.
  Applies to: Attributes
  Rationale: An address table obviously needs address fields. But an order table with embedded address fields should reference the address table instead.
  Example: If the "account" table has columns "address_line_1", "city", "state", "zip_code" AND a separate "address" table exists in the model, those address columns on "account" are denormalized — replace them with an "address_id" FK. But if the table IS the "address" table, those columns obviously belong there. And if no separate address table exists anywhere in the model, the embedded columns might be the only source of that data — keep them.

G05-R009: Geographic coordinates (latitude, longitude) are denormalized ONLY on tables that are NOT physical or spatial entities. A warehouse, store, or facility legitimately owns its coordinates.
  Applies to: Attributes
  Rationale: Physical entities have inherent locations. Their coordinates are intrinsic properties, not copies from a location table.
  Example: "latitude" and "longitude" on the "warehouse" table are the warehouse's own physical location — keep them. Same for "store", "facility", "branch", "sensor", or "plant" — these are physical things with inherent locations. But "latitude" and "longitude" on a "customer" table or "invoice" table are likely copied from a location entity and should be replaced with a "location_id" FK.

G05-R010: Columns like customer_name or customer_code are denormalized ONLY when the customer table exists AND a customer_id foreign key already exists in the same table.
  Applies to: Attributes
  Rationale: If the FK exists, the name/code can be obtained through a join and should not be stored redundantly. But if there is no FK, the column might be the only reference and should be kept.
  Example: If the "order" table has "customer_id" (FK to customer) AND "customer_name", then "customer_name" is redundant — it can be retrieved by joining on "customer_id". Remove it. But if the "order" table has "customer_name" with NO "customer_id" FK, then "customer_name" might be the only link to the customer. In that case, the problem is a missing FK (orphaned reference), not denormalization.

G05-R012: Only columns ending with the standard primary key suffix (like _id) should be considered as potential orphaned foreign keys. Columns ending in _code, _number, or _reference are identifiers, not foreign keys.
  Applies to: Attributes
  Rationale: A country_code or batch_number is a business identifier, not a relationship to another table. Treating them as FKs leads to incorrect linking.
  Example: "warehouse_id" ending in "_id" is a candidate for an orphaned FK — investigate if a "warehouse" table exists. But "country_code", "batch_number", "external_reference", "currency_code", and "legacy_ref" should NOT be treated as FK candidates, even though they look like identifiers. They are business codes or system references, not relationships to other tables in the model.

G05-R013: During attribute deduplication, only choose among existing attribute names. Never introduce a new attribute name that does not already exist in the table.
  Applies to: Attributes
  Rationale: Deduplication should reduce redundancy, not create new naming artifacts. Inventing new names during dedup introduces untested, unvalidated identifiers that may not match business glossary terms.
  Example: If a table has both "termination_date" and "end_date" and they are semantically the same, pick ONE of those two existing names — do not create a new name like "conclusion_date" or "close_date" that nobody asked for. Similarly, if "created_at" and "creation_date" are duplicates, choose one of those exact names, not "date_created" or "record_creation_timestamp".

G05-R014: When flagging denormalization violations, only flag a column when its name prefix EXACTLY matches a table name in the model. Do not guess synonyms. A column called "client_phone" is NOT a violation just because a "customer" table exists — only "customer_phone" would be a candidate.
  Applies to: Attributes
  Rationale: Synonym guessing leads to false positives that wrongly remove columns. An exact prefix match is the only reliable signal that a column is denormalized from a specific table.
  Example: "customer_name" on the "order" table is a valid denormalization candidate because the prefix "customer" exactly matches the "customer" table. But "client_name" on the "order" table is NOT a candidate even though "client" might be a synonym for "customer" — there is no "client" table in the model. Similarly, "member_email" is NOT a violation just because a "customer" table exists. Only exact table name matches count.

G05-R015: Attributes that reference non-existent products (orphan attributes with broken domain.product references) must be removed from the model.
  Applies to: Attributes
  Rationale: An attribute claiming to belong to a product that does not exist is structurally invalid. Keeping it creates phantom metadata that confuses every consumer and breaks model traversal.
  Example: If the "billing" domain was removed during consolidation but an attribute still references "billing.invoice", that attribute is orphaned — it belongs to a product that no longer exists. Similarly, if a table "customer_preference" was merged into "customer.profile", any remaining attribute referencing "customer.customer_preference" is orphaned and must be removed since the target no longer exists.

G06 - DOMAIN & DIVISION RULES
================================================================================
How to organize domains and classify them into divisions.

G06-R001: Each domain MUST be classified into exactly ONE division: operations, business, or corporate.
  Applies to: Domains, Divisions
  Rationale: Division classification enables balanced model design and helps prioritize which domains to build first. A domain cannot serve two divisions.
  Example: In a retail company, "inventory" and "logistics" belong to the operations division, "customer" and "billing" belong to the business division, and "hr" and "finance" belong to the corporate division. In healthcare, "clinical" and "pharmacy" are operations, "patient" and "billing" are business, and "compliance" and "hr" are corporate. Each domain fits exactly one division. NOTE: Domain names should use terminology natural to the specific industry being modeled.

G06-R002: Operations and Business division domains together must make up at least 80% of all domains. Equivalently, Corporate (supporting) division domains must not exceed 20%.
  Applies to: Domains, Divisions
  Rationale: A data model should focus on what the business DOES (operations) and WHO it serves (business). Corporate functions are supporting actors, not the main story. Over-representing HR, finance, and legal dilutes focus.
  Example: In a model with 10 domains, at least 8 should be operations or business domains (like logistics, production, customer, billing, product, sales, fulfillment, quality), and at most 2 should be corporate (like hr, finance). A model with 3 operations, 2 business, and 5 corporate domains is severely unbalanced — the corporate support functions are drowning out the actual business.

G06-R004: Do not add any corporate/supporting domain until the model has at least 2 operations domains AND at least 2 business domains.
  Applies to: Domains, Divisions
  Rationale: The foundation must be solid before adding supporting structures. A model with 3 corporate domains and 1 operations domain has its priorities backward.
  Example: You must first establish at least 2 operations domains (say "logistics" and "production") and 2 business domains (say "customer" and "billing") before adding any corporate domain like "hr" or "finance". A model that starts with "hr", "legal", and "finance" before establishing the core operational and commercial domains has its priorities completely wrong.

G06-R005: When selecting domains, alternate between operations and business divisions first (pick one from each, repeat), then add corporate domains last.
  Applies to: Domains, Divisions
  Rationale: Interleaved selection ensures both operational and commercial perspectives are represented before supporting functions are considered.
  Example: The selection order should alternate: (1) an operations domain, (2) a business domain, (3) another operations domain, (4) another business domain, and so on. Only after operations and business are well-covered do you add corporate domains like "hr" or "finance". This interleaving ensures the model is balanced at every step. NOTE: Domain names should reflect the actual industry being modeled.

G06-R006: Each core business concept (customer, product, invoice, etc.) must be owned by exactly ONE domain. This is the Single Source of Truth principle applied to domain ownership.
  Applies to: Domains
  Rationale: If both 'customer' domain and 'client' domain claim to own customer data, every consumer must decide which to trust. One owner eliminates this ambiguity.
  Example: The concept of "customer identity" must be owned by exactly one domain — say the "customer" domain. Having both a "customer" domain and a "client" domain that both claim to own person data is a violation. Similarly, "product" should be owned by one domain (e.g., "product" or "catalog"), and "invoice" by one domain (e.g., "billing"). Other domains that need this data must reference it via FK. NOTE: The domain name should use the industry-appropriate term (customer, patient, policyholder, guest, etc.).

G06-R007: Every domain must have foreign key relationships connecting it to at least one other domain. No domain should be an island.
  Applies to: Domains, Relations
  Rationale: An isolated domain cannot participate in cross-domain analytics, which is the whole point of an enterprise data model.
  Example: The "procurement" domain must have at least one FK relationship to another domain — perhaps "purchase_order.supplier_id" referencing the "vendor" domain's supplier table, or "purchase_order_line.product_id" referencing the "product" domain. A domain with zero cross-domain FKs (no tables referencing it, and it referencing nothing outside itself) is a data silo that cannot participate in enterprise analytics.

G06-R008: Tables cannot be relocated across divisions. An operations table stays in operations; a business table stays in business.
  Applies to: Products, Domains, Divisions
  Rationale: Division boundaries reflect fundamentally different business functions. Moving a table across divisions would change its semantic meaning.
  Example: An "equipment" table in the operations-division "operations" domain cannot be relocated to the business-division "customer" domain. Operations tables can only move to other operations domains, business tables to other business domains, and corporate tables to other corporate domains. Moving a "facility" table (operations) to the "finance" domain (corporate) is absolutely forbidden.

G06-R010: Tables that are eponymous with their domain (inventory.inventory, workforce.employee) must NEVER be relocated to a different domain.
  Applies to: Products, Domains
  Rationale: The domain was likely NAMED after this entity. Removing it would undermine the domain's entire reason for existence.
  Example: "inventory.inventory" cannot be relocated — the "inventory" domain was named after the inventory entity. "workforce.employee" cannot move — the workforce domain exists because of employees. "finance.budget" is the reason the finance domain exists. If you remove the eponymous table, the domain name no longer makes sense and loses its purpose.

G06-R011: Child tables stay with their parent. Never relocate a child table away from the domain where its parent table lives.
  Applies to: Products, Relations
  Rationale: Parent-child relationships form a logical unit. Splitting them across domains breaks the natural hierarchy and complicates joins.
  Example: If "order" lives in the "sales" domain, then "order_item" (its child via order_item.order_id FK) must also stay in the "sales" domain. You cannot move "order_item" to the "product" domain while leaving "order" in "sales". When relocating a table, all its FK-related children must move together as a unit.

G06-R012: Tables that are heavily referenced by 3 or more other domains should not be relocated, as they serve as central hubs in the model.
  Applies to: Products, Relations
  Rationale: Moving a hub table would break references from many domains at once and create widespread cascading changes.
  Example: If the "customer.profile" table is referenced by tables in the "billing", "sales", "service", and "support" domains (4 domains), it is a hub table and must not be relocated. Moving it would require updating FK references across all four domains, creating widespread cascading changes and potential breakage.

G06-R013: Tables in a 'shared' domain should be moved to their natural owner domain, unless they are cross-cutting reference data used by 3 or more domains.
  Applies to: Products, Domains
  Rationale: 'Shared' is a parking lot, not a permanent home. Most tables have a natural business owner. Only truly universal reference data should stay shared.
  Example: "shared.rate_schedule" that is only used by the "billing" domain should be moved to "billing.rate_schedule" — it has a natural owner. But "shared.country" or "shared.currency" that are referenced by 5+ domains (billing, customer, sales, procurement, shipping) should stay in "shared" because they are truly cross-cutting reference data with no single natural owner.

G06-R014: Domain names must represent real business functions. Generic catch-all names like utilities, infrastructure, services, platform, shared, common, core, base, general, misc, and admin are forbidden.
  Applies to: Domains
  Rationale: If you cannot point to a real department or team with that name on an org chart, it is not a legitimate business domain. It is a lazy catch-all.
  Example: Instead of "utilities", use specific business units like "powergeneration" or "watertreatment". Instead of "infrastructure", use "networkoperations" or "datacenter". Instead of "services", specify the type: "fieldservice" or "customerservice". Instead of "admin", use "facilitymanagement" or "officeservices". The domain name must describe a real, recognizable business function.

G06-R015: No analytics, reporting, insights, intelligence, or data warehouse domains. The data model represents operational and transactional business data, not derived analytics.
  Applies to: Domains
  Rationale: Analytics belong in a separate layer (Gold in medallion architecture). The data model captures what happened, not what it means.
  Example: Domain names like "analytics", "reporting", "intelligence", "insights", "metrics", "kpi", "dashboard", "bi", or "datawarehouse" are all forbidden. These represent the Silver/Gold analytics layer, not the operational data model. If you need a domain for usage data, call it "usage" (the raw events), not "usage_analytics" (the derived metrics).

G06-R016: Every domain must pass the 'Org Chart Test': would a department, team, or division with this name actually exist in the organization?
  Applies to: Domains
  Rationale: If you can imagine a VP or Director leading this domain, it represents a real business function. If you cannot, it is too abstract.
  Example: "finance" passes the test — every company has a Finance Department. "customer" passes — there is a Customer Success or Customer Relations team. "inventory" passes for a retailer — there is an Inventory Management team. But "utilities" fails — nobody says "I work in the Utilities department." "processing" fails — it is too vague. Ask: "Would a recruiter post jobs in the [domain_name] department?"

G06-R017: If two proposed domains would share 30% or more of the same tables, they should be merged into one domain.
  Applies to: Domains
  Rationale: High overlap means the domains are not distinct business functions. Merging eliminates confusion about which domain owns which concepts.
  Example: If a proposed "customer" domain and a proposed "client" domain would both need tables for profile, contact, address, and account (sharing 4 out of 6 tables = 67% overlap), they must be merged into a single domain. Ask: "Would a data consumer be confused about which domain owns the person concept?" If yes, merge them.

G06-R018: Do not create a 'shared' domain during initial model design. If consolidation is needed, it happens as a later optimization step.
  Applies to: Domains
  Rationale: During initial design, every table should be placed in its authoritative business domain. Pre-emptive sharing creates ambiguity.
  Example: When designing the initial model, place customer identity in the "customer" domain and "address" in whichever domain naturally owns it — do NOT preemptively create a "shared" domain to house cross-cutting tables. A downstream consolidation phase will detect overlapping tables across domains (60%+ overlap) and automatically merge them into a "shared" domain with proper discriminator columns. Trust the process; do not shortcut it.

G06-R019: Domains with fewer tables than the configured minimum products per domain (default 5) may be merged into a related domain. Only dynamically created domains are eligible — user-defined or protected domains are never merged.
  Applies to: Products, Domains
  Rationale: A domain with only 1-2 tables usually represents a concept too narrow to justify its own domain. Merging it into a related domain reduces fragmentation while respecting explicit user decisions.
  Example: If the configured minimum is 5 tables per domain and the "warranty" domain has only 2 tables ("warranty_claim" and "warranty_policy"), it may be merged into the "service" domain that handles related post-sale concepts. But if the user explicitly defined "warranty" as a required domain, it is protected and stays regardless of table count. The merge decision considers domain affinity — tables move to the most semantically related neighbor.

G07 - DATA TYPE RULES
================================================================================
What data types to use for columns.

G07-R001: Use standard SQL types: STRING, BIGINT, INT, DECIMAL (with precision and scale), TIMESTAMP, DATE, BOOLEAN.
  Applies to: Attributes
  Rationale: Standard types ensure portability across platforms and clarity for anyone reading the schema.
  Example: Use "STRING" for names and descriptions, "BIGINT" for primary keys and foreign keys, "DECIMAL(18,2)" for monetary amounts like price or balance, "DATE" for birth dates or due dates, "TIMESTAMP" for event times like created_at or modified_at, and "BOOLEAN" for flags like is_active or is_deleted. These types work consistently across Spark SQL, BigQuery, Snowflake, and other platforms.

G07-R002: Do not use complex types like ARRAY, STRUCT, or MAP for column types.
  Applies to: Attributes
  Rationale: Complex types break normalization, complicate joins, and are not consistently supported across SQL engines. Model nested data as separate tables.
  Example: Instead of storing phone numbers as ARRAY<STRING> on the customer table, create a separate "phone_number" table with "customer_id" FK and one row per phone. Instead of storing address as STRUCT<street, city, zip>, create a separate "address" table. Instead of MAP<STRING, STRING> for custom attributes, create a "custom_attribute" table with key-value columns. Nested data becomes normalized separate tables.

G07-R008: Every table must be classified with a data type: master_data (core entities), reference_data (lookup/classification data), transactional_data (business events), or association_data (many-to-many junction tables).
  Applies to: Products
  Rationale: Classification communicates the table's purpose, update frequency, and expected access patterns to every consumer.
  Example: "customer" and "product" are master_data — core entities that change slowly and represent authoritative "golden records." "country" and "currency" are reference_data — static lookup tables used for classification. "order" and "payment" are transactional_data — business events that grow rapidly and capture what happened at a point in time. "enrollment" (linking students to courses) is association_data — a junction table representing a many-to-many relationship.

G07-R010: Boolean representation should be consistent across the model. Choose one format (true/false, Y/N, or 1/0) and apply it everywhere.
  Applies to: Attributes
  Rationale: Mixing boolean formats within the same model creates confusion and requires consumers to handle multiple formats.
  Example: If the model uses BOOLEAN type with true/false, then every boolean column ("is_active", "is_deleted", "has_email_opt_in") must use true/false. Do not have "is_active" using true/false while "has_consent" uses Y/N and "is_verified" uses 1/0. Pick one format at the start and enforce it across every table in the entire model.

G07-R011: When no data type is explicitly specified for an attribute, infer the type from the attribute name pattern. Suffix _id implies BIGINT. Suffixes _date or _dt imply DATE. Suffixes _timestamp, _ts, _at, or _time imply TIMESTAMP. Suffixes _amount, _price, _cost, _balance, _total, or _fee imply DECIMAL(18,2). Suffixes _count, _qty, or _quantity imply INT. Prefixes is_ or has_, or suffix _flag imply BOOLEAN. Suffixes _pct, _percent, _ratio, or _weight imply DECIMAL(10,4). All other names default to STRING.
  Applies to: Attributes
  Rationale: Consistent type inference from naming conventions prevents type mismatches and ensures the model is structurally sound even when types are not explicitly declared.
  Example: "employee_id" → BIGINT (ends with _id). "hire_date" → DATE (ends with _date). "created_at" → TIMESTAMP (ends with _at). "last_login_timestamp" → TIMESTAMP (ends with _timestamp). "invoice_amount" → DECIMAL(18,2) (ends with _amount). "line_item_count" → INT (ends with _count). "is_active" → BOOLEAN (starts with is_). "active_flag" → BOOLEAN (ends with _flag). "discount_pct" → DECIMAL(10,4) (ends with _pct). "allocation_ratio" → DECIMAL(10,4) (ends with _ratio). "customer_name" → STRING (default).

G07-R012: Categorical attributes must define their allowed values using pipe-separated format in the value_regex field. Each value represents one allowed option.
  Applies to: Attributes
  Rationale: Pipe-separated enumeration provides a clear, machine-readable constraint on what values are allowed. It prevents inconsistencies like "Active" vs "active" vs "ACTIVE" across records.
  Example: An attribute "order_status" with allowed values should define value_regex as "pending|confirmed|shipped|delivered|cancelled". An attribute "payment_method" should define it as "credit_card|debit_card|bank_transfer|cash|digital_wallet". An attribute "priority_level" should define it as "low|medium|high|critical". Each pipe-separated token is one discrete allowed value — no wildcards, no ranges, no regex special characters.

G08 - TAG & CLASSIFICATION RULES
================================================================================
How to classify data sensitivity and apply tags.

G08-R001: Data classification goes in tags only, NEVER in column names. Use tags like 'restricted,pii_email', not column names like 'restricted_email'.
  Applies to: Attributes
  Rationale: Column names describe WHAT the data is. Tags describe HOW it should be handled. Mixing them pollutes the namespace and makes names harder to read.
  Example: Name the column "email" and tag it "restricted,pii_email" — not "restricted_email" or "pii_email_address". Name the column "phone_number" and tag it "restricted,pii_phone" — not "confidential_phone". The column name describes the business content (email, phone); the tag describes the sensitivity classification. Keep these two concerns separate.

G08-R002: Only add classification tags for CONFIDENTIAL or RESTRICTED data. Regular operational data (status codes, amounts, dates) should have empty tags.
  Applies to: Attributes
  Rationale: Over-tagging creates noise. If everything is tagged, nothing stands out. Tags should highlight data that needs special treatment.
  Example: "email" gets tagged "restricted,pii_email" (sensitive PII). "credit_card_number" gets tagged "restricted,pii_financial" (PCI data). But "status", "order_date", "quantity", "country_code", and "is_active" should have empty tags — they are regular operational data that require no special handling. Tagging everything would make the truly sensitive columns harder to identify.

G08-R003: Personally Identifiable Information (PII) — names, emails, phone numbers, social security numbers, addresses — MUST be tagged as RESTRICTED with a specific PII tag (for example, restricted,pii_email).
  Applies to: Attributes
  Rationale: PII has legal and regulatory implications (GDPR, CCPA). Explicit tagging ensures proper handling, access control, and compliance reporting.
  Example: Customer name gets "restricted,pii_name". Email gets "restricted,pii_email". Phone number gets "restricted,pii_phone". National ID or SSN gets "restricted,pii_ssn". Physical address gets "restricted,pii_address". Date of birth gets "restricted,pii_dob". In any industry, identifiers that can trace back to an individual (account numbers, device identifiers, loyalty IDs) should get appropriate PII tags.

G08-R004: Protected Health Information (PHI) and Payment Card Industry data (PCI) MUST be tagged as RESTRICTED.
  Applies to: Attributes
  Rationale: PHI and PCI data are subject to strict regulatory requirements (HIPAA, PCI-DSS). Tags enable automated compliance enforcement.
  Example: PHI data — medical records, health conditions, prescriptions, treatment history, insurance claims, disability status — must all be tagged "restricted,pii_health". PCI data — credit card numbers, CVV, PIN, cardholder data, payment tokens, bank account numbers — must be tagged "restricted,pii_financial". These regulatory categories (HIPAA for PHI, PCI-DSS for PCI) carry severe penalties for non-compliance, making accurate tagging critical.

G08-R005: Do NOT include structural tags like 'primary_key' or 'foreign_key' in classification tags. Classification tags are about data sensitivity, not data structure.
  Applies to: Attributes
  Rationale: Structural metadata (PK/FK status) and classification metadata (PII/restricted) serve different purposes and should not be mixed.
  Example: A column's tags should be "restricted,pii_email" — NOT "restricted,pii_email,primary_key" or "foreign_key,restricted". The fact that a column is a PK or FK is structural metadata tracked separately. Tags are exclusively for data sensitivity classification. Adding "primary_key" or "foreign_key" to the tags field mixes two unrelated concepts and confuses automated policy enforcement.

G08-R006: Tags should be comma-separated. For example: restricted,pii_email or confidential,pii_financial.
  Applies to: Attributes
  Rationale: A consistent format makes tags parseable and enables automated policy enforcement.
  Example: Correct tag formats: "restricted,pii_email", "restricted,pii_phone", "confidential,pii_financial", "restricted,pii_ssn". The first element is the classification level, the second is the specific PII category. Do not use semicolons ("restricted;pii_email"), pipes ("restricted|pii_email"), or spaces ("restricted pii_email"). The comma-separated format enables automated parsing and policy engines.

G08-R007: Standard classification levels are: restricted (highest sensitivity), confidential, internal, and public (lowest sensitivity).
  Applies to: Attributes
  Rationale: A consistent classification hierarchy enables uniform access control policies across the entire model.
  Example: "restricted" = highest sensitivity (PII like SSN, credit cards, health records — needs encryption, access logging, and strict authorization). "confidential" = sensitive business data (salary grades, pricing strategies, IP addresses — limited access). "internal" = general business data (visible to all employees but not outside). "public" = openly accessible (published product names, company address). Most operational columns (status, dates, codes) do not need any tag at all.

G08-R009: Use the authoritative classification matrix for common data types: customer names are restricted/pii_name, emails are restricted/pii_email, phone numbers are restricted/pii_phone, financial accounts are restricted/pii_financial, IP addresses are confidential/pii_ip, and operational status fields have no tags.
  Applies to: Attributes
  Rationale: A predefined matrix eliminates subjectivity and ensures consistent classification across all teams and all tables.
  Example: Follow this matrix consistently: customer name = "restricted,pii_name". Email = "restricted,pii_email". Phone number = "restricted,pii_phone". National ID = "restricted,pii_national_id". Credit card number = "restricted,pii_financial". Bank account = "restricted,pii_financial". IP address = "confidential,pii_ip". Device serial number = "confidential,pii_device". Manager name = empty (business reference, not direct PII). Cost center code = empty. Status/state = empty. This matrix must be applied uniformly — no exceptions, no judgment calls.

G08-R010: Individual tag values must not contain reserved characters: dots (.), commas (,), hyphens (-), equals (=), slashes (/), colons (:), or whitespace. Use underscores instead.
  Applies to: Attributes
  Rationale: Commas are used as tag separators (per G08-R006). Other reserved characters conflict with parsing, metadata formats, or query syntax. Consistent underscore-only tags ensure tags are machine-parseable and unambiguous.
  Example: "pii_email" is valid — underscores only. "pii-email" is invalid — contains a hyphen. "pii.email" is invalid — contains a dot. "pii email" is invalid — contains a space. "data=sensitive" is invalid — contains an equals sign. Always convert reserved characters to underscores: "pii-email" becomes "pii_email", "data.classification" becomes "data_classification".

G09 - RELATIONSHIP & GRAPH RULES
================================================================================
How the data model's relationships must be structured.

G09-R001: The data model must form a Directed Acyclic Graph (DAG). In plain terms: if you follow FK relationships from any table, you must NEVER arrive back at the starting table. Cycles are forbidden.
  Applies to: Relations
  Rationale: Cycles make it impossible to determine insert/delete order, break referential integrity enforcement, and create infinite loops in graph traversal.
  Example: If "order" references "customer" (via customer_id), and "customer" references "account" (via account_id), and "account" references "order" (via latest_order_id) — following the chain order -> customer -> account -> order brings you back to the start. This is a cycle and is forbidden. The fix: remove the weakest link, "account.latest_order_id", which is a computed convenience reference, not a structural necessity.

G09-R002: Bidirectional links (table A references B AND table B references A) are the most dangerous cycles and the highest priority to detect and eliminate.
  Applies to: Relations
  Rationale: Bidirectional links are the simplest and most common form of cycle. They make both tables mutually dependent, which breaks all load and delete operations.
  Example: "invoice.account_id" references "account", AND "account.primary_invoice_id" references "invoice" — this is a bidirectional link. "enrollment.profile_id" references "profile", AND "profile.active_enrollment_id" references "enrollment" — another bidirectional link. In both cases, analyze the cardinality: an account has MANY invoices, so keep "invoice.account_id" (N:1) and remove "account.primary_invoice_id" (computed 1:1 convenience reference).

G09-R003: Every table must have at least one relationship — either referencing another table (outgoing FK) or being referenced by another table (incoming FK). No completely disconnected tables allowed.
  Applies to: Products, Relations
  Rationale: A disconnected table cannot participate in joins and represents a data silo. If it has no relationships, it either does not belong in the model or a relationship was missed.
  Example: A "promotion" table with no outgoing FKs (it does not reference any other table) and no incoming FKs (no other table references it) is a siloed table — it is completely disconnected from the rest of the model. It must either gain a relationship (e.g., "order.promotion_id" referencing it, or "promotion.product_id" referencing the product table) or be removed from the model. Note: a "country" table that only has incoming FKs (many tables reference it, but it references nothing) is NOT siloed — it is a valid reference table.

G09-R004: Every domain must connect to at least one other domain through foreign key relationships. No isolated domains.
  Applies to: Domains, Relations
  Rationale: An isolated domain is a data silo at the domain level. Cross-domain relationships enable enterprise-wide analytics.
  Example: The "procurement" domain must have at least one cross-domain FK — perhaps "purchase_order.supplier_id" referencing a supplier in the "vendor" domain, or "purchase_order_line.product_id" referencing the "product" domain. If all tables within "procurement" only reference each other and no table outside "procurement" references them, the entire domain is an island that cannot participate in enterprise-wide queries like "which products did we procure for which vendors?"

G09-R005: Foreign keys should follow the child-to-parent direction. The child table holds the FK column, not the parent. For example, order.customer_id (child references parent), never customer.latest_order_id (parent references child).
  Applies to: Relations
  Rationale: In a one-to-many relationship, the 'many' side (child) references the 'one' side (parent). The reverse direction implies the parent knows about a specific child, which is semantically wrong.
  Example: A customer can have MANY orders, so "order.customer_id" (child references parent) is correct. "customer.latest_order_id" (parent references one specific child) is wrong — it loses information because a customer has multiple orders, not just one. Similarly, keep "payment.invoice_id" (child to parent), not "invoice.last_payment_id" (parent to child). Keep "enrollment.profile_id", not "profile.active_enrollment_id".

G09-R006: When breaking cycles, NEVER break the core ownership FK (child referencing parent). Instead, break the weaker reference (convenience, computed, or derived links).
  Applies to: Relations
  Rationale: The child-to-parent FK is the structural backbone. Breaking it destroys the fundamental hierarchy. Convenience references like 'latest_order_id' or 'current_address_id' are expendable.
  Example: If a cycle exists between "order" and "customer" via "order.customer_id" (structural N:1) and "customer.latest_order_id" (computed 1:1), break "customer.latest_order_id" — it is a convenience reference. NEVER break "order.customer_id" because it is the core ownership link. Similarly, breaking "order_item.order_id" (child to parent) would destroy data integrity — always break the weaker computed reference instead.

G09-R007: When breaking cycles, prefer breaking FKs that follow patterns like latest_, current_, default_, primary_, or active_ — these are computed references, not structural relationships.
  Applies to: Relations
  Rationale: These column name patterns indicate derived or convenience references that point to one specific child row. They are added for performance, not structural integrity, and are safe to remove.
  Example: "customer.latest_order_id" — break it (computed, points to one specific order). "account.primary_invoice_id" — break it (convenience reference). "profile.active_subscription_id" — break it (derived, can be queried dynamically). "invoice.last_payment_id" — break it. "agent.current_ticket_id" — break it. All of these follow the pattern of a parent storing a pointer to one specific child, which is always a computed/derived reference that is safe to remove.

G09-R008: NEVER break primary identity links (like profile_id referencing profile), financial ownership links (like payment referencing invoice), or core transaction chains.
  Applies to: Relations
  Rationale: These relationships form the business logic backbone. Breaking them would make the model logically incorrect, even if it resolves a cycle.
  Example: Never break "enrollment.profile_id" referencing "profile" — it is a primary identity link. Never break "payment.invoice_id" referencing "invoice" — it is a financial ownership link. Never break "billing_account_id" referencing "billing_account" or "contract_id" referencing "contract". Never break "order_item.order_id" referencing "order" — it is a core parent-child transaction chain. These are the business logic backbone; removing any of them would make the model logically incorrect.

G09-R009: When multiple cycles share the same edge, breaking that one edge can resolve all of them. Minimize the number of unique breaks.
  Applies to: Relations
  Rationale: Each break weakens the model. A single strategic break that resolves multiple cycles is always preferable to multiple individual breaks.
  Example: If 10 cycles all pass through the edge "customer.latest_order_id -> order", breaking that one edge resolves all 10 cycles at once. The strategy is: (1) group cycles by shared edges, (2) identify the ONE edge that appears in the most cycles, (3) break that edge, (4) verify which cycles are resolved. Do not propose 10 separate breaks for 10 cycles when one well-chosen break could handle them all.

G09-R010: Breaking a cycle must NEVER create a new disconnected (siloed) table or domain.
  Applies to: Relations
  Rationale: Solving one problem (cycle) by creating another (disconnected table) makes the model worse, not better.
  Example: If breaking "payment.invoice_id" would leave the "payment" table with no remaining FK relationships (no outgoing FKs and no incoming FKs from other tables), then that break is forbidden — it would create a siloed table. After every proposed cycle break, verify that no table or domain becomes completely disconnected. If a break would create a silo, choose a different edge to break.

G09-R012: Classify each FK relationship into one of three types: true FK (structural, never break), business reference (safe to break if needed), or computed reference (always break first).
  Applies to: Relations
  Rationale: Not all FKs are equal. Knowing the type helps make informed decisions about which relationships to sacrifice when resolving cycles.
  Example: "order_item.order_id -> order" is a true FK (structural child-to-parent, never break). "order.region_code -> region" is a business reference (lookup, safe to break if a cycle requires it). "customer.latest_order_id -> order" is a computed reference (derived convenience pointer, always break first). When resolving cycles, break computed references first, business references second, and true FKs never.

G09-R013: Lookup tables that only have incoming FKs (other tables reference them, but they reference nothing) are NOT considered siloed. They are valid reference tables.
  Applies to: Products, Relations
  Rationale: A country or status_code table is designed to be referenced by others. It does not need outgoing FKs to be useful.
  Example: A "country" table with "country_id" and "country_name" has no outgoing FKs — it does not reference any other table. But many tables reference it (customer.country_id, address.country_id, supplier.country_id). This is NOT siloed — it is a valid reference/lookup table by design. Same applies to "currency", "status_code", and "unit_of_measure" tables. They are leaf nodes in the graph that only receive incoming references.

G11 - QUALITY & VALIDATION RULES
================================================================================
Quality standards the data model must meet.

G11-R007: The number of domains must fall within the configured minimum and maximum range.
  Applies to: Domains
  Rationale: Too few domains means the model is too coarse to be useful. Too many means it is fragmented with overlapping concerns.
  Example: For a medium-sized model, the configured range might be 5 to 10 domains. A model with only 2 domains ("operations" and "business") is too coarse — it lumps too many concepts together. A model with 25 domains is too fragmented — many domains would overlap and confuse consumers. If the count falls outside the range, either consolidate overlapping domains or split overly broad ones.

G11-R008: The number of tables per domain must fall within the configured minimum and maximum range.
  Applies to: Products, Domains
  Rationale: A domain with 1 table is probably too narrow. A domain with 100 tables probably needs splitting. The range ensures balanced domain sizing.
  Example: If the configured range is 5 to 10 tables per domain, a domain with only 1 table is too narrow and should probably be merged into a related domain. A domain with 30 tables likely covers too many concepts and should be split into more focused domains. The "customer" domain with 7 tables (profile, account, address, contact, preference, segment, loyalty) is well within range.

G11-R009: The number of columns per table must fall within the configured minimum and maximum range.
  Applies to: Products, Attributes
  Rationale: A table with 2 columns is likely incomplete. A table with 200 columns is likely denormalized or combining multiple concepts.
  Example: If the configured range is 10 to 25 columns per table, a "customer" table with only a PK and a name (2 columns) is incomplete — it needs contact info, status, dates, etc. A "customer" table with 150 columns is likely a denormalized mega-table combining customer, address, account, and preference data that should be split into separate tables. A well-designed table typically falls within the suggested range.

G11-R013: Domain names representing technical infrastructure (logging, etl, integration, audit_trail, batch_control) are forbidden.
  Applies to: Domains
  Rationale: The data model represents business concepts, not technical plumbing. ETL pipelines and log tables belong in a different layer.
  Example: Domain names "logging", "etl", "integration", "audit_trail", "batch_control", "system", "technical", "data", "info", and "api" are all forbidden because they represent technical infrastructure, not business functions. An ETL pipeline belongs in the data engineering layer. A logging system belongs in the observability layer. The business data model should only contain domains a business user would recognize.

G11-R019: Every domain must have a description of at least 20 characters that clearly explains its business purpose. No two domains may have descriptions with more than 70% word overlap.
  Applies to: Domains
  Rationale: Short descriptions provide no guidance on what belongs in the domain. Overlapping descriptions indicate domains are not sufficiently differentiated and may need to be merged.
  Example: "finance" with description "Handles money" (13 chars) fails — too short. "finance" with description "Covers all financial transactions, accounts receivable, accounts payable, and general ledger operations" passes — 97 chars, clearly defines scope. If "billing" has "Manages customer invoices, payments, and billing cycles" and "finance" has "Manages customer invoices, payments, and financial records", the overlap exceeds 70% — one domain must be redefined to have a distinct purpose.

G11-R020: Every table (product) must have a description that explains its business purpose and what kind of data it stores.
  Applies to: Products
  Rationale: Tables without descriptions are undiscoverable and unmaintainable. A description is the first thing someone reads to understand if a table is relevant to their use case.
  Example: An "order" table should have a description like "Tracks purchase transactions between the company and customers, including order details, fulfillment status, and payment terms." A table with an empty description or a single-word description like "orders" fails this rule — it tells nobody what the table represents.

G11-R021: Every column (attribute) must have a description that explains what business data it captures.
  Applies to: Attributes
  Rationale: Column names alone are often ambiguous. "amount" on its own could be invoice amount, payment amount, credit amount, or tax amount. The description eliminates ambiguity.
  Example: "effective_date" should have a description like "The date from which this record's data becomes valid and active in the system." A column "status" with no description is ambiguous — status of what? A description like "The current lifecycle state of the claim (open, under_review, approved, denied, closed)" makes it unambiguous.

G11-R022: The model must contain at least 3 domains to be considered meaningful, regardless of any configuration overrides.
  Applies to: Domains
  Rationale: A single domain or two domains usually means the business problem has not been decomposed into its constituent areas. Even simple businesses have distinct areas (e.g., customers, products, transactions).
  Example: A model with only a "general" domain containing all 20 tables fails — the business is not decomposed. A model with "customer" and "product" domains (only 2) also fails — where do the transactions, billing, and shipping concepts live? Minimum 3 domains forces meaningful decomposition, e.g., "customer", "product", "sales".

G11-R023: Domains that end up with zero products (tables) must be removed from the model. Protected or user-defined domains are the only exception.
  Applies to: Products, Domains
  Rationale: An empty domain clutters the model and signals poor planning. If no table belongs to a domain, the domain has no reason to exist.
  Example: If after table deduplication and merging the "logistics" domain has zero remaining tables, it must be removed — not left as an empty shell. But if the user explicitly defined "logistics" as a required domain in their input, it is protected and stays even if temporarily empty.

G11-R024: The total domain count must never exceed twice the configured maximum (max_domains × 2). This is an absolute ceiling regardless of any other consideration.
  Applies to: Domains
  Rationale: Even with flexibility in domain count, an upper bound prevents runaway domain proliferation. More than double the planned maximum creates an unmanageable, fragmented model.
  Example: If the configured max_business_domains is 6, the absolute ceiling is 12 domains. A model with 15 domains would be rejected. If max is 10, the ceiling is 20. This ceiling catches cases where domain generation overshoots significantly — for instance, creating 18 domains when the plan called for at most 8. The model must be consolidated before proceeding.

G12 - PRODUCT DESIGN RULES
================================================================================
How to design tables (data products) for the model.

G12-R001: Tables must represent business concepts ONLY. Never create tables for technical artifacts like logging, ETL processing, integration staging, or system configuration.
  Applies to: Products
  Rationale: The data model serves business users and analytics. Technical tables belong in separate operational schemas, not the business model.
  Example: Valid business tables: "customer", "order", "invoice", "product", "contract". Forbidden technical tables: "etl_job_log", "error_log", "integration_staging", "system_config", "batch_control", "audit_trail", "data_snapshot", "temp_processing". If the table name contains words like "log", "etl", "staging", "temp", "archive", "config", or "batch", it is a technical artifact that belongs in an operational schema, not the business data model.

G12-R002: No analytics, reporting, aggregation, summary, or KPI tables. The model captures raw operational and transactional data. Derived metrics belong in a separate analytics layer.
  Applies to: Products
  Rationale: Mixing raw data with derived data creates confusion about what is authoritative. The model stores facts; analytics layers compute insights.
  Example: Forbidden tables: "fraud_detection", "churn_prediction", "revenue_analysis", "sales_summary", "performance_metrics", "usage_statistics", "cdr_analysis". Also forbidden: any table ending in "_analysis", "_analytics", "_report", "_summary", "_aggregate", "_dashboard", "_metrics", "_kpi", "_score", or "_prediction". Store the raw events (orders, payments, usage records) and let the analytics layer compute the insights.

G12-R003: No pre-calculated metric columns (like average_monthly_spend, total_lifetime_value, churn_score). Store the raw component data and let the analytics layer compute aggregations.
  Applies to: Attributes
  Rationale: Pre-calculated metrics become stale, require maintenance, and duplicate logic that belongs in the analytics layer. Raw data is the single source of truth.
  Example: Do NOT include columns like "total_revenue" (computed sum of charges), "average_monthly_spend" (computed average), "lifetime_value_score" (derived metric), or "churn_score" (prediction output). Instead, store the raw component columns: "subscription_charge", "usage_charge", "tax_amount" — these are the real business data. The analytics layer computes totals, averages, and scores from these raw components.

G12-R004: No speculative or futuristic columns. Only include columns for capabilities the business currently has, not aspirational features.
  Applies to: Attributes
  Rationale: Speculative columns add clutter, mislead users into thinking the capability exists, and require maintenance for something that may never be built.
  Example: Do NOT include columns like "blockchain_hash", "digital_twin_id", "ai_prediction_score", "metaverse_avatar_id", or "quantum_encryption_key" unless the business explicitly has these capabilities today. If a strategic initiative is still aspirational or future-looking, do not model columns for it. Only include attributes for capabilities that are currently implemented and generating data.

G12-R006: Every table must be assigned a division: operations (core mechanisms), business (revenue and customer-facing), corporate (governance and compliance), or supporting (back-office functions).
  Applies to: Products, Divisions
  Rationale: Division assignment connects the table to the organizational structure and helps prioritize development and governance efforts.
  Example: Operations division: "network_element", "service_provisioning", "inventory" — core technical/physical mechanisms. Business division: "customer", "billing", "marketing", "sales" — commercialization and customer-facing activities. Corporate division: "regulatory", "compliance" — high-level governance. Supporting division: "hr", "finance", "procurement" — back-office functions that enable the business but do not directly generate revenue.

G12-R007: Every table must be classified as core (essential, directly queried by stakeholders) or helper (supporting entity that enables core tables).
  Applies to: Products
  Rationale: This distinction helps teams prioritize data quality efforts and determine which tables need the highest level of governance.
  Example: Core tables: "customer" (stakeholders directly query customer data), "invoice" (finance team queries billing data), "order" (sales team queries order data). Helper tables: "address" (supports customer with location data but rarely queried alone), "contact_method" (supports customer with phone/email), "order_status_history" (supports order with historical changes). Core tables get the highest governance priority.

G12-R008: Core tables are fundamental entities the business cannot function without. They cannot be merged or removed. Examples: customer, invoice, product.
  Applies to: Products
  Rationale: Core tables are the identity of the business model. Removing or merging them would be like removing the foundation of a building.
  Example: "customer.profile" IS the source of truth for customer identity — it cannot be merged into a shared table or removed. "billing.invoice" IS the source of truth for billing — it cannot be merged or deleted. "sales.order" IS the source of truth for orders. These tables represent unique, irreplaceable business concepts. They are protected from any merge, relocation to shared, or removal operation regardless of overlap percentages.

G12-R009: Each domain should have 1 to 3 core tables maximum.
  Applies to: Products, Domains
  Rationale: If everything is core, nothing is. Limiting core designation ensures the label carries real meaning and focuses governance efforts.
  Example: The "customer" domain might have 7 tables but only 1-2 are core: "profile" (core) and perhaps "account" (core), while "address", "contact_method", "preference", "segment", and "loyalty" are helpers. The "billing" domain might designate "invoice" as its single core table. Not every domain needs a core table — a "shared" domain with only reference/lookup tables might have none.

G12-R010: Association (junction) tables for many-to-many relationships should not exceed 15% of total business tables.
  Applies to: Products, Relations
  Rationale: Excessive junction tables indicate over-engineering of relationships. Most real-world relationships are one-to-many and should use simple FKs.
  Example: In a model with 40 business tables, association tables should not exceed 6 (15% of 40). If you have 10 junction tables (25%), you are likely over-engineering — many of those relationships could be simplified to one-to-many FKs. Review each junction table and ask: "Does the business truly operate with many-to-many here AND track data about the relationship?" If not, simplify to a direct FK.

G12-R011: Use many-to-many (M:N) relationships ONLY when the business actually operates that way AND tracks data about the relationship itself (like dates, roles, amounts).
  Applies to: Products, Relations
  Rationale: M:N is more complex to query and maintain. It should only be used when the business reality genuinely requires it, not as a default.
  Example: "Students enroll in multiple courses, and the business tracks grades and enrollment dates" — this requires a M:N junction table "enrollment" with columns like grade, enrollment_date, and status. "Employees work on multiple projects with tracked roles and hours" — this requires an "assignment" junction table with role, hours_allocated, and start_date. The key test: does the business have a NAME for this relationship AND does it track at least 2 attributes about the relationship? If yes, use M:N.

G12-R012: Use a simple one-to-many FK instead of M:N when any of these are true: a FK already exists, the relationship is ownership-based, either table is reference data, or there is no relationship-specific data to track.
  Applies to: Relations
  Rationale: Simple FKs are easier to query, maintain, and understand. M:N should be the exception, not the rule.
  Example: "An order belongs to one customer" — use a simple FK "order.customer_id", not a junction table. "A product has one category" — use "product.category_id", not a product_category junction. "An employee belongs to one department" — use "employee.department_id". Ownership semantics ("belongs to"), reference/lookup relationships, and cases where you are only linking IDs with no additional data all call for simple FKs, never M:N.

G12-R013: Cross-domain many-to-many relationships are very rare. Expect at most 0 to 1 per model.
  Applies to: Relations
  Rationale: Cross-domain M:N relationships add significant complexity. In most business models, cross-domain connections are one-to-many.
  Example: Most cross-domain connections are one-to-many: an order (sales domain) references one customer (customer domain) via a simple FK. A M:N junction table spanning two domains — like a "supplier_product" table linking the "procurement" domain to the "product" domain — is extremely rare. If your model has 3 or more cross-domain junction tables, re-evaluate whether those relationships truly need to be M:N or could be simplified to direct FKs.

G12-R014: Simple properties (status, type, category, flag) should be embedded as columns in the table. Only create separate tables for complex entities that have their own attributes.
  Applies to: Products, Attributes
  Rationale: Creating a separate 'status' table with 3 rows is over-engineering. Embed simple values directly. Reserve separate tables for entities with real depth.
  Example: Embed directly as columns: "status" (active/inactive/suspended), "type" (residential/commercial), "category" (premium/standard/basic), "is_active" (true/false). Do NOT create a separate "order_status" table with 3 rows just to hold active/completed/cancelled. But DO create a separate "product" table — a product is a complex entity with its own name, description, price, and category attributes. The test: if it has only a code and a label, embed it; if it has 5+ meaningful attributes, it deserves its own table.

G12-R015: Tables that represent fundamentally different business workflows should be separate entities, even if they seem related. For example, order (sales workflow) and service_request (support workflow) should be distinct tables.
  Applies to: Products
  Rationale: Different workflows have different lifecycles, SLAs, and business rules. Combining them creates a confusing mega-table.
  Example: "order" belongs to the commercial sales workflow (sales SLAs, revenue recognition). "service_request" belongs to the support/assurance workflow (support SLAs, resolution tracking). Even though both involve customers and dates, they go through completely different business processes. Ask: "Do these go through the same business process?" If no, they are separate tables. Do not combine them into a generic "request" mega-table.

G12-R016: Distinguish between catalog/template entities and instance entities. A product_catalog defines what is available; an enrollment represents what a customer actually has. These are different tables.
  Applies to: Products
  Rationale: Catalog data describes possibilities; instance data describes reality. They have different lifecycles, different consumers, and different update patterns.
  Example: "product_catalog" (what IS available — product definitions, pricing templates) and "enrollment" (what a customer HAS — active agreements, placed orders) are different tables even though they share similar attributes. The catalog is updated by the product team infrequently; enrollments are created by customers constantly. Similarly, "rate_schedule" (pricing template) and "charge" (actual billed amount) are different entities — one describes the rules, the other describes what happened.

G12-R017: Every column must have clear, direct business value. Remove filler columns that exist only to pad the schema.
  Applies to: Attributes
  Rationale: Each column carries maintenance cost (documentation, quality checks, storage). If a column cannot answer a specific business question, it should not exist.
  Example: Every column should pass this test: "What specific business question does this column answer?" "order_date" answers "When was this order placed?" — keep it. "notes_field_3" answers nothing specific — remove it. "misc_data" is a filler column with no clear purpose — remove it. If you cannot articulate clear business value for a column, it is padding the schema and should not exist.

G12-R018: Certain table types must include fundamental columns. Person entities MUST include name and contact fields. Account entities MUST include type, status, and key dates. Transaction entities MUST include date, status, and amount.
  Applies to: Products, Attributes
  Rationale: A customer table without a name or a transaction table without a date is useless. These are the absolute minimum for any entity of that type.
  Example: A "customer" (person entity) MUST include identifying name field(s), a primary contact method (email or phone), and a status. A "payment" (transaction entity) MUST include a transaction date, a status, and an amount. A "contract" (account entity) MUST include a type, a status, and key dates (start_date, end_date). A customer table with only a PK and no name is useless; a payment table with no date or amount is equally useless.

G12-R019: The model must provide comprehensive customer coverage. B2C businesses need individual customer entities. B2B businesses need corporate account entities. Mixed businesses need both.
  Applies to: Products
  Rationale: Missing a major customer segment means an entire class of business queries cannot be answered. The model must represent ALL customer types the business serves.
  Example: A retail company (B2C) needs individual customer entities like personal profiles and loyalty accounts. A professional services firm (B2B) needs corporate customer entities like enterprise accounts and business hierarchies. A bank serving both individuals and businesses (mixed B2B/B2C) MUST have entities supporting BOTH — individual customers AND corporate accounts. Missing either segment means an entire class of business queries ("Who are our corporate clients?") cannot be answered. NOTE: Use industry-appropriate terminology for customer entities.

G12-R020: Small tables with very few columns should be evaluated: MERGE them into a parent table if their columns logically belong there, KEEP them if they are valid lookup/reference tables, DROP them only if they are exact semantic duplicates.
  Applies to: Products
  Rationale: A 3-column table might be a legitimate reference table or might be an accidental fragment that belongs in another table. Evaluate case by case.
  Example: A "customer_type" table with only PK, code, and label (3 columns) — KEEP it, it is a valid lookup/reference table. A "customer_phone" table with PK, customer_id, and phone_number (3 columns) that exists alongside a "customer" table that already has room for contact data — MERGE its columns into the customer table. A "client_profile" table that is an exact semantic duplicate of "customer.profile" — DROP it. Always prefer KEEP or MERGE over DROP; only drop proven exact duplicates.

G12-R021: Columns within a table must follow a strict ordering: primary key first, foreign keys second, business attributes third, housekeeping columns fourth, and history-tracking columns last.
  Applies to: Attributes
  Rationale: Consistent column ordering makes tables scannable and predictable. Anyone reading a DDL or data dictionary immediately knows where to find the PK, the relationships, and the audit fields without searching.
  Example: For an "order" table, correct ordering is: (1) order_id (PK), (2) customer_id, product_id (FKs), (3) order_date, total_amount, status, shipping_method (business attributes), (4) is_deleted, created_by, updated_by (housekeeping), (5) created_at, updated_at, effective_date, version (history-tracking). An "order" table where "created_at" appears between "order_date" and "total_amount" violates this rule — housekeeping columns must not be interleaved with business attributes.

G12-R022: Association (junction) tables that have only FK columns and no additional business attributes must include at minimum audit columns: created_at (TIMESTAMP) and created_by (STRING).
  Applies to: Products, Attributes
  Rationale: Even a pure junction table needs to record when and who created the relationship. Without audit columns, there is no traceability for when linkages were established.
  Example: A "course_enrollment" association table linking students and courses might have only: enrollment_id (PK), student_id (FK), course_id (FK). Since it has no business attributes, it must also include "created_at" and "created_by" at minimum. This way, you know when each enrollment record was created and by which system or user.

G12-R023: Housekeeping columns are standard supporting columns for record lifecycle and audit. They include: is_deleted, is_active, is_enabled, is_archived, deleted_at, deleted_date, deleted_by, created_by, updated_by, modified_by, batch_id, load_id, and similar ETL/audit tracking columns. These must be placed after business attributes in column order.
  Applies to: Attributes
  Rationale: Housekeeping columns are infrastructure-level metadata, not business data. Separating them from business attributes keeps the business-meaningful columns prominent and easy to find.
  Example: In a "customer" table, business attributes like "name", "email", "phone", "segment" come first. Then housekeeping columns follow: "is_deleted" (BOOLEAN — soft delete flag), "is_active" (BOOLEAN — whether the customer is currently active), "created_by" (STRING — who created the record), "updated_by" (STRING — who last modified it). The housekeeping columns never appear between "name" and "email" — they always come after all business attributes.

G12-R024: History-tracking columns are standard temporal columns for versioning and time-based validity. They include: created_at, updated_at, modified_at, effective_date, effective_from, effective_to, valid_from, valid_to, expiry_date, end_date, is_current, is_latest, current_flag, version_number, row_start_date, and row_end_date. These must be placed after housekeeping columns (last position in column order).
  Applies to: Attributes
  Rationale: History-tracking columns support slowly changing dimensions and temporal queries. Placing them last keeps them separate from both business logic and housekeeping metadata.
  Example: In a "price_schedule" table, column order is: (1) price_schedule_id (PK), (2) product_id (FK), (3) base_price, currency, discount_pct (business), (4) is_active, created_by (housekeeping), (5) effective_from, effective_to, is_current, version_number (history-tracking). The "effective_from" and "is_current" columns go at the very end, not mixed in with "base_price" and "currency".

G12-R025: Attribute semantic synonyms must be resolved to one canonical name per table. When multiple attributes in the same table represent the same concept under different names, keep only one. Standard synonym groups include: termination_date/end_date/expiry_date, creation_date/created_at/created_date, modification_date/updated_at/modified_date, start_date/effective_date/begin_date, created_by/creator/created_by_user, is_deleted/deleted/is_removed, and threshold_unit/unit_of_measure.
  Applies to: Attributes
  Rationale: Multiple names for the same concept within one table create confusion about which column to use and can lead to data inconsistencies where the "same" data is stored in two columns with different values.
  Example: If a table has both "end_date" and "expiry_date", and they mean the same thing, keep ONE — do not keep both. If a table has "created_at" (TIMESTAMP) and "creation_date" (DATE), these are semantic synonyms representing the same concept at different precision — keep only the more precise one ("created_at"). If a table has "is_deleted" and "deleted" (both BOOLEAN), they are synonyms — keep "is_deleted" which follows the is_ prefix convention.

G12-R026: A table is classified as "small" when it has fewer attributes than the configured minimum (default 5). Small tables must be evaluated: MERGE into a parent if columns belong there, KEEP if it is a valid reference/lookup table, DROP only if it is an exact semantic duplicate.
  Applies to: Products, Attributes
  Rationale: The threshold makes the small-table evaluation from G12-R020 concrete and measurable. Without a defined threshold, "small" is subjective and inconsistently applied.
  Example: If the configured minimum is 5 attributes per table, a "customer_type" table with 3 attributes (PK, code, label) is small — evaluate it. It is a valid reference table, so KEEP it. A "customer_phone" table with 3 attributes (PK, customer_id, phone_number) is small — evaluate it. Its columns belong on the customer table, so MERGE. A "status_lookup" table with 2 attributes that duplicates another reference table — DROP it. Any table with 5 or more attributes is NOT small and is not subject to this evaluation.

G12-R027: A new table should only be created to resolve unlinked FK references when at least 2 different existing tables reference the same missing concept. A single FK reference from only one table is insufficient evidence — it is likely a hallucinated or incorrect FK.
  Applies to: Products, Relations
  Rationale: Creating a table from a single FK reference risks introducing phantom tables based on malformed data. Requiring references from 2+ sources provides cross-validation that the concept genuinely needs its own table.
  Example: If both "order.warehouse_id" and "shipment.warehouse_id" reference a "warehouse" entity that does not yet exist, that is strong evidence — create the "warehouse" table. But if only "order.legacy_warehouse_id" references "warehouse" and no other table does, it is likely an incorrect or stale FK — do not create a phantom "warehouse" table based on a single reference. Wait until multiple tables corroborate the need.

SEMANTIC DISTINCTION RULES (Supplementary)
================================================================================
Rules that prevent false-positive duplicate detection across the model.

G02-SD01: Method vs Channel are NOT duplicates. payment_method (credit card, bank transfer) and payment_channel (web, mobile app, store) capture different dimensions.
  Applies to: Attributes
  Rationale: Method describes the INSTRUMENT used. Channel describes the INTERFACE used. They answer different analytical questions and should coexist.
  Example: "payment_method" holds values like "credit_card", "bank_transfer", "cash", "digital_wallet" — it describes WHAT instrument was used to pay. "payment_channel" holds values like "web", "mobile_app", "in_store", "phone" — it describes WHERE or HOW the payment was made. A customer could pay by credit card (method) through the mobile app (channel). These answer different business questions: "Which payment methods have the highest fraud?" vs "Which channels generate the most revenue?"

G02-SD02: ID vs Name are NOT duplicates. customer_id (system identifier) and customer_name (display value) serve different purposes.
  Applies to: Attributes
  Rationale: IDs enable machine processing and joins. Names enable human readability. Every entity typically needs both.
  Example: "customer_id" (like 10042) is a system-generated numeric identifier used for joins and machine processing. "customer_name" (like "Sarah Mitchell") is a human-readable display value. Similarly, "product_code" (like "PLN-50GB-PRE") is a lookup key, while "product_name" (like "Premium Wireless Plan 50GB") is the human-readable name. Both columns serve fundamentally different purposes and must coexist — one for machines, one for humans.

G02-SD03: Target vs Actual are NOT duplicates. sla_target_time (what was promised) and sla_actual_time (what was delivered) answer different business questions.
  Applies to: Attributes
  Rationale: Comparing target to actual is a fundamental business analysis pattern. Removing either makes the comparison impossible.
  Example: "sla_target_time" (4 hours — what was promised to the customer) and "sla_actual_time" (3.5 hours — how long it actually took) are NOT duplicates. Similarly, "budget_amount" (what was planned) and "actual_amount" (what was spent) are distinct. "target_delivery_date" (promised) and "actual_delivery_date" (delivered) are distinct. Comparing target to actual is a fundamental business analysis pattern — removing either column makes the comparison impossible.

G02-SD04: Lifecycle timestamps are NOT duplicates. created_timestamp, modified_timestamp, approved_timestamp, and completed_timestamp each represent a distinct business event.
  Applies to: Attributes
  Rationale: Each timestamp marks a different milestone in the entity's lifecycle. They enable workflow analysis and audit trails.
  Example: An order might have: "created_timestamp" (when the order was placed), "approved_timestamp" (when it was reviewed and approved), "shipped_timestamp" (when it left the warehouse), and "completed_timestamp" (when the customer received it). Each marks a different business milestone. However, "created_date" and "creation_date" ARE duplicates — they represent the SAME event with slightly different names. Only flag as duplicates when columns represent the same event, not different lifecycle stages.

G02-SD05: Different date granularities can coexist. A scheduled_date (DATE for day-level planning) and scheduled_start_time (TIMESTAMP for precise timing) serve different precision needs.
  Applies to: Attributes
  Rationale: Some consumers need day-level granularity for reporting; others need exact timestamps for scheduling. Both are valid.
  Example: "scheduled_date" (DATE type, like 2025-03-15) is used for day-level planning and reporting — "How many deliveries are scheduled for next Tuesday?" "scheduled_start_time" (TIMESTAMP type, like 2025-03-15T09:30:00) is used for precise scheduling — "What exact time should the driver arrive?" A logistics manager needs the DATE for daily planning; a dispatcher needs the TIMESTAMP for exact timing. Both columns serve different precision needs and should coexist.
